**Tskhinvali** ([Georgian](/wiki/Georgian_language "Georgian language"): ცხინვალი [\[ˈt͡sʰχinʷali\]](/wiki/Help:IPA/Georgian "Help:IPA/Georgian") [](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/0/02/Tskhinvali.ogg/Tskhinvali.ogg.mp3 "Play audio")^[ⓘ](/wiki/File:Tskhinvali.ogg "File:Tskhinvali.ogg")^) or **Tskhinval** ([Ossetian](/wiki/Ossetian_language "Ossetian language"): Цхинвал, Чъреба, romanized: *Cxinval, Čreba*,
Ossetian pronunciation: [\[t͡sχinˈvɒɫ, ˈt͡ʃʼɾʲebɑ\]](/wiki/Help:IPA "Help:IPA") [](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/4/4b/Tskhinval.ogg/Tskhinval.ogg.mp3 "Play audio")^[ⓘ](/wiki/File:Tskhinval.ogg "File:Tskhinval.ogg")^; Russian: Цхинвал(и), [romanized](/wiki/Romanization_of_Russian "Romanization of Russian"): *Tskhinval(i)*, [\[tsxʲɪnˈval(ʲɪ)\]](/wiki/Help:IPA/Russian "Help:IPA/Russian")) is the capital of the disputed *de facto* independent [Republic of South Ossetia](/wiki/Republic_of_South_Ossetia "Republic of South Ossetia"), internationally considered part of [Shida Kartli](/wiki/Shida_Kartli "Shida Kartli"), [Georgia](/wiki/Georgia_(country) "Georgia (country)") (except by the [Russian Federation](/wiki/Russian_Federation "Russian Federation") and [four other UN member states](/wiki/International_recognition_of_Abkhazia_and_South_Ossetia "International recognition of Abkhazia and South Ossetia")). Tskhinvali Region, known historically as [Samachablo](/wiki/Samachablo "Samachablo"), was always part of the Georgian state as a single military and administrative entity.^[\[3\]](#cite_note-3)^^\[*[neutrality](/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view "Wikipedia:Neutral point of view") is [disputed](/wiki/Talk:Tskhinvali#"was_always_part_of_the_Georgian_state_as_a_single_military_and_administrative_entity." "Talk:Tskhinvali")*\]^ It is located on the [Great Liakhvi River](/wiki/Great_Liakhvi_River "Great Liakhvi River") approximately 100 kilometres (62 mi) northwest of the Georgian capital [Tbilisi](/wiki/Tbilisi "Tbilisi").{#cite_ref-3}  

Name {#Name}
------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Tskhinvali&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Name")\]

The name of **Tskhinvali** is derived from the [Old Georgian](/wiki/Old_Georgian "Old Georgian") *Krtskhinvali* ([Georgian](/wiki/Georgian_language "Georgian language"): ქრცხინვალი), from earlier *Krtskhilvani* ([Georgian](/wiki/Georgian_language "Georgian language"): ქრცხილვანი), literally meaning "the land of [hornbeams](/wiki/Hornbeam "Hornbeam")",^[\[4\]](#cite_note-4)^^[\[5\]](#cite_note-5)^ which is the historical name of the city.^[\[6\]](#cite_note-6)^ See [ცხინვალი](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%83%AA%E1%83%AE%E1%83%98%E1%83%9C%E1%83%95%E1%83%90%E1%83%9A%E1%83%98 "wikt:ცხინვალი") for more.{#cite_ref-6}

From 1934 to 1961, the city was named **Staliniri** ([Georgian](/wiki/Georgian_language "Georgian language"): სტალინირი, [Ossetian](/wiki/Ossetian_language "Ossetian language"): Сталинир), which was a compilation of [Joseph Stalin](/wiki/Joseph_Stalin "Joseph Stalin")'s surname with Ossetian word "Ir" which means [Ossetia](/wiki/Ossetia "Ossetia"). Modern [Ossetians](/wiki/Ossetians "Ossetians") call the city **Tskhinval** (leaving off the final "i", which is a [nominative case](/wiki/Nominative_case "Nominative case") ending in Georgian); the other Ossetian name of the city is **Chreba** ([Ossetian](/wiki/Ossetian_language "Ossetian language"): Чъреба) which is only spread as a [colloquial](/wiki/Colloquialism "Colloquialism") word.^[\[7\]](#cite_note-7)^ The name Chreba comes from the [Georgian](/wiki/Georgian_language "Georgian language") *Ḳreba* ([Georgian](/wiki/Georgian_language "Georgian language"): კრება), literally meaning "gathering" due to the city historically serving as a trading point.^[\[8\]](#cite_note-8)^{#cite_ref-8}  

History {#History}
------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Tskhinvali&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: History")\]

The area around the present-day Tskhinvali was first populated back in the [Bronze Age](/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age"). The unearthed settlements and archaeological artifacts from that time are unique in that they reflect influences from both [Iberian](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Iberia_(antiquity) "Kingdom of Iberia (antiquity)") (east Georgia) and [Colchian](/wiki/Colchis "Colchis") (west Georgia) cultures with possible [Sarmatian](/wiki/Sarmatian "Sarmatian") elements.
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Tskhinvali._Rudnev%2C_D_1886.jpg/220px-Tskhinvali._Rudnev%2C_D_1886.jpg)](/wiki/File:Tskhinvali._Rudnev,_D_1886.jpg) A vintage photo of Tskhinvali by D. Rudnev, 1886.

Tskhinvali was first chronicled by Georgian sources in 1398 as a village in [Kartli](/wiki/Kartli "Kartli") (central Georgia) though a later account credits the 3rd century AD Georgian king [Aspacures II of Iberia](/wiki/Aspacures_II_of_Iberia "Aspacures II of Iberia") with its foundation as a fortress. By the early 18th century, Tskhinvali was a small "royal town" populated chiefly by monastic serfs. Tskhinvali was annexed to the [Russian Empire](/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire") along with the rest of eastern Georgia in 1801. Located on a [trade route](/wiki/Trade_route "Trade route") which linked [North Caucasus](/wiki/North_Caucasus "North Caucasus") to Tbilisi and [Gori](/wiki/Gori,_Georgia "Gori, Georgia"), Tskhinvali gradually developed into a commercial town with a mixed [Georgian Jewish](/wiki/Georgian_Jews "Georgian Jews"), [Georgian](/wiki/Georgians "Georgians"), [Armenian](/wiki/Armenians "Armenians") and [Ossetian](/wiki/Ossetians "Ossetians") population. In 1917, it had 600 houses with 38.4% [Georgian Jews](/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Georgia "History of the Jews in Georgia"), 34.4% Georgians, 17.7% Armenians and 8.8% Ossetians.^[\[9\]](#cite_note-9)^{#cite_ref-9}

The town saw [clashes](/wiki/Georgian-Ossetian_conflict_(1918%E2%80%931920) "Georgian-Ossetian conflict (1918–1920)") between Georgian People's Guard and pro-[Bolshevik](/wiki/Bolsheviks "Bolsheviks") Ossetian peasants during the 1918--20 period, when Georgia gained [brief independence](/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Georgia "Democratic Republic of Georgia") from Russia. [Soviet](/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") rule was established by the [invading](/wiki/Red_Army_invasion_of_Georgia "Red Army invasion of Georgia") [Red Army](/wiki/Red_Army "Red Army") in March 1921, and a year later, in 1922, Tskhinvali was made a capital of the [South Ossetian Autonomous Oblast](/wiki/South_Ossetian_Autonomous_Oblast "South Ossetian Autonomous Oblast") within the [Georgian SSR](/wiki/Georgian_SSR "Georgian SSR"). Subsequently, the town became largely Ossetian due to intense urbanisation and Soviet [Korenizatsiya](/wiki/Korenizatsiya "Korenizatsiya") ("nativization") policy which induced an inflow of the Ossetians from the nearby rural areas into Tskhinvali. It was essentially an industrial centre, with lumber mills and manufacturing plants, and had also several cultural and educational institutions such as a venerated Pedagogical Institute (currently Tskhinvali State University) and a drama theatre. According to the last [Soviet census](/wiki/Soviet_Census_(1989) "Soviet Census (1989)") (in 1989), Tskhinvali had a population of 42,934, and according to the census of Republic of South Ossetia in 2015, the population was 30,432 people.

During the acute phase of the [Georgian-Ossetian conflict](/wiki/Georgian-Ossetian_conflict "Georgian-Ossetian conflict"), Tskhinvali was a scene of ethnic tensions and ensuing armed confrontation between Georgian and Ossetian forces. The 1992 [Sochi](/wiki/Sochi "Sochi") ceasefire accord left Tskhinvali in the hands of [Ossetians](/wiki/Ossetians "Ossetians").  

### Russo-Georgian War {#Russo-Georgian_War}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Tskhinvali&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Russo-Georgian War")\]  
Main article: [Battle of Tskhinvali](/wiki/Battle_of_Tskhinvali "Battle of Tskhinvali")
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Pmt.jpg/220px-Pmt.jpg)](/wiki/File:Pmt.jpg) The monument to the victims of the Georgian-Ossetian conflict near [the Armenian church](/wiki/St._Mary%27s_Church,_Tskhinvali "St. Mary's Church, Tskhinvali") in Tskhinvali

A considerable part of the population of South Ossetia (at least, 30,000 out of 70,000) fled into [North Ossetia--Alania](/wiki/North_Ossetia%E2%80%93Alania "North Ossetia–Alania") prior or immediately after the start of the 2008 war.^[\[10\]](#cite_note-10)^ However, many civilians were killed during the shelling and the following [Battle of Tskhinvali](/wiki/Battle_of_Tskhinvali "Battle of Tskhinvali") (162 civilian deaths were documented by the Russian team of investigators^[\[11\]](#cite_note-11)^ and 365 -- by the South Ossetian authorities^[\[12\]](#cite_note-12)^). The town was heavily damaged during the battle due to extensive shelling by the [Georgian Army](/wiki/Georgian_Army "Georgian Army"). [Andrey Illarionov](/wiki/Andrey_Illarionov "Andrey Illarionov") visited the town in October 2008, and reported that Jewish Quarter indeed was in ruins, though he observed that the ruins were overgrown with shrubs and trees, which indicates that the destruction took place during the [1991--1992 South Ossetia War](/wiki/1991%E2%80%931992_South_Ossetia_War "1991–1992 South Ossetia War").^[\[13\]](#cite_note-13)^ However, [Mark Ames](/wiki/Mark_Ames "Mark Ames"), who was covering the last war for [The Nation](/wiki/The_Nation "The Nation"), stated that Tskhinvali's main residential district, nicknamed Shanghai because of its population density (it's where most of the city's high-rise apartment blocks are located), and the old Jewish Quarter, were completely destroyed.^[\[14\]](#cite_note-14)^{#cite_ref-14}

### Climate {#Climate}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Tskhinvali&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Climate")\]

Located in the [Caucasus](/wiki/Caucasus "Caucasus"), at 860 metres (2,820 ft) above sea level, Tskhinvali has a [humid continental climate](/wiki/Humid_continental_climate "Humid continental climate") ([Köppen](/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification "Köppen climate classification"): *Dfb* ), with an average annual precipitation of 805 millimetres (31.7 in). Summers are mild and winters are cold, with [snowfalls](/wiki/Snow "Snow").

Present {#Present}
------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Tskhinvali&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Present")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Valery_Gergiev_in_Tskhinval_21_August_2008.jpg/220px-Valery_Gergiev_in_Tskhinval_21_August_2008.jpg)](/wiki/File:Valery_Gergiev_in_Tskhinval_21_August_2008.jpg) 21 August 2008. [Valery Gergiev](/wiki/Valery_Gergiev "Valery Gergiev") with [Mariinsky Theatre](/wiki/Mariinsky_Theatre "Mariinsky Theatre") opera in Tskhinvali.

Currently, Tskhinvali functions as the capital of [South Ossetia](/wiki/South_Ossetia "South Ossetia"). Before the 2008 war it had a population of approximately 30,000.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^ The town remained significantly impoverished in the absence of a permanent political settlement between the two sides in the past two decades.

On 21 August 2008, a world-known^[\[16\]](#cite_note-16)^ Russian conductor and director of the [Mariinsky Theatre](/wiki/Mariinsky_Theatre "Mariinsky Theatre"), of Ossetian origin, [Valery Gergiev](/wiki/Valery_Gergiev "Valery Gergiev") conducted a concert near the ruined building of [South Ossetian parliament](/wiki/Parliament_of_South_Ossetia "Parliament of South Ossetia") in memory of the Ossetian victims of the [Russo-Georgian War](/wiki/Russo-Georgian_War "Russo-Georgian War").^[\[17\]](#cite_note-17)^{#cite_ref-17}
