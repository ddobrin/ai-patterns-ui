**Sukhumi** or **Sokhumi** is a city in a wide bay on the [Black Sea](/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea")'s eastern coast. It is both the [capital](/wiki/Capital_city "Capital city") and largest city of the Republic of [Abkhazia](/wiki/Abkhazia "Abkhazia"), a partially recognised state that most countries consider a part of [Georgia](/wiki/Georgia_(country) "Georgia (country)"). The city has been controlled by Abkhazia since the [Abkhazian war](/wiki/War_in_Abkhazia_(1992%E2%80%931993) "War in Abkhazia (1992–1993)") in 1992--93. The city, which has an [airport](/wiki/Sukhumi_Dranda_Airport "Sukhumi Dranda Airport"), is a port, major rail junction and a holiday resort because of its beaches, [sanatoriums](/wiki/Sanatorium_(resort) "Sanatorium (resort)"), mineral-water spas and semitropical climate. It is also a member of the [International Black Sea Club](/wiki/International_Black_Sea_Club "International Black Sea Club").^[\[2\]](#cite_note-3)^{#cite_ref-3}

Sukhumi's history can be traced to the 6th century BC, when it was settled by Greeks, who named it Dioscurias. During this time and the subsequent Roman period, much of the city disappeared under the Black Sea. The city was named Tskhumi when it became part of the [Kingdom of Abkhazia](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Abkhazia "Kingdom of Abkhazia") and then the [Kingdom of Georgia](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Georgia "Kingdom of Georgia"). Contested by local princes, it became part of the [Ottoman Empire](/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") in the 1570s, where it remained until it was conquered by the [Russian Empire](/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire") in 1810.

After a period of conflict during the [Russian Civil War](/wiki/Russian_Civil_War "Russian Civil War"), it briefly became part of the [Democratic Republic of Georgia](/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Georgia "Democratic Republic of Georgia"),^[\[3\]](#cite_note-4)^ until it was again taken by the [Bolsheviks](/wiki/Bolsheviks "Bolsheviks"). Within the [Soviet Union](/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"), Sukhumi served as the capital of the [Socialist Soviet Republic of Abkhazia](/wiki/Socialist_Soviet_Republic_of_Abkhazia "Socialist Soviet Republic of Abkhazia") and then the [Abkhaz Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic](/wiki/Abkhaz_Autonomous_Soviet_Socialist_Republic "Abkhaz Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic") within the [Georgian SSR](/wiki/Georgian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic "Georgian Soviet Socialist Republic"). It was also a popular holiday destination. As the [Soviet Union dissolved](/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union "Dissolution of the Soviet Union") in the early 1990s, the city suffered significant damage during the [Abkhaz--Georgian conflict](/wiki/Abkhaz%E2%80%93Georgian_conflict "Abkhaz–Georgian conflict"). The present-day population of 60,000 is only half of the population living there toward the end of Soviet rule.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^{#cite_ref-4}  

Toponym {#Toponym}
------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Sukhumi&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Toponym")\]

In [Georgian](/wiki/Georgian_language "Georgian language"), the city is known as *Sokhumi* (სოხუმი), amongst [Samurzakanians](/wiki/Samurzakanians "Samurzakanians") in [Megrelian](/wiki/Megrelian_language "Megrelian language") the city is sometimes referred to as *Aqujikha* (აყუჯიხა),^[\[4\]](#cite_note-5)^^[\[5\]](#cite_note-Chirikba-6)^ and in Russian as Сухум (*Sukhum* ) or Сухуми (*Sukhumi* ). The toponym Sokhumi derives from the Georgian word *Tskhomi/Tskhumi* (ცხომი/ცხუმი), which in turn is supposed to be derived from [Svan](/wiki/Svan_language "Svan language") [tskhum (ცხუმ)](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%83%AA%E1%83%AE%E1%83%A3%E1%83%9B#Svan "wikt:ცხუმ") meaning "[hornbeam](/wiki/Hornbeam "Hornbeam") tree".^[\[6\]](#cite_note-Etymology-7)^ In Abkhaz, the city is known as *Aqwa* (Аҟәа) which is believed to derive from *a-qwara* (а-ҟәара), meaning "stony seashore".^[\[5\]](#cite_note-Chirikba-6)^ According to Abkhaz tradition *Aqwa* (Аҟәа) signifies *water* .^[\[7\]](#cite_note-colarusso_mp54-8)^{#cite_ref-colarusso_mp54_8-0}

Medieval Georgian sources knew the town as *Tskhumi* (ცხუმი).^[\[8\]](#cite_note-9)^^[\[9\]](#cite_note-10)^^[\[10\]](#cite_note-11)^ Later, under [Ottoman](/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") control, the town was known in Turkish as *Suhum-Kale* ([Ottoman Turkish](/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish_language "Ottoman Turkish language"): صخوم قلعه), which was derived from the earlier Georgian form Tskhumi or read to mean "Tskhumi fortress".^[\[11\]](#cite_note-goltzgd056-12)^^[\[12\]](#cite_note-13)^{#cite_ref-13}

The ending -i in the above forms represents the Georgian nominative suffix. The town was officially called Сухум (*Sukhum* ) in Russian until 16 August 1936, when this was changed to *Sukhumi* (Сухуми).^[\[13\]](#cite_note-14)^ This remained so until 4 December 1992, when the Supreme Council of Abkhazia restored the previous version.^[\[14\]](#cite_note-post1992-15)^ Russia also readopted its official spelling in 2008,^[\[15\]](#cite_note-16)^ though Сухуми is also still being used.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^{#cite_ref-16}

In English, the most common form today is *Sukhumi* ,^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^ although *Sokhumi* is increasing in usage and has been adopted by sources including *[United Nations](/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations")* ,^[\[16\]](#cite_note-17)^ *[Encyclopædia Britannica](/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica "Encyclopædia Britannica")* ,^[\[17\]](#cite_note-18)^ *[MSN Encarta](/wiki/MSN_Encarta "MSN Encarta")* ,^[\[18\]](#cite_note-19)^ [Esri](/wiki/Esri "Esri")^[\[19\]](#cite_note-20)^ and [Google Maps](/wiki/Google_Maps "Google Maps").^[\[20\]](#cite_note-21)^{#cite_ref-21}  

History {#History}
------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Sukhumi&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: History")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Colchis_coin.jpg/220px-Colchis_coin.jpg)](/wiki/File:Colchis_coin.jpg) Coin of Dioscurias, late 2nd century BC. Obverse: The caps ([pilei](/wiki/Pileus_(hat) "Pileus (hat)")) of [Dioscuri](/wiki/Dioscuri "Dioscuri") surmounted by stars; reverse: [Thyrsos](/wiki/Thyrsos "Thyrsos"), ΔΙΟΣΚΟΥΡΙΑΔΟΣ

The history of the city began in the mid-6th century BC when an earlier settlement of the second and early first millennia BC, frequented by local [Colchian tribes](/wiki/Colchian_tribes "Colchian tribes"), was replaced by the [Milesian](/wiki/Miletus "Miletus") [Greek colony](/wiki/Colonies_in_antiquity "Colonies in antiquity") of *Dioscurias* ([Greek](/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language"): Διοσκουριάς).^[\[21\]](#cite_note-arrian-original-22)^^[\[22\]](#cite_note-arrian-translation-23)^ The city is said to have been founded^[\[23\]](#cite_note-24)^^[\[24\]](#cite_note-25)^ and named by the [Dioscuri](/wiki/Castor_and_Pollux "Castor and Pollux"), the twins Castor and Pollux of [classical mythology](/wiki/Classical_mythology "Classical mythology"). According to another legend it was founded by Amphitus and Cercius of [Sparta](/wiki/Sparta "Sparta"), the charioteers of the Dioscuri.^[\[25\]](#cite_note-26)^^[\[26\]](#cite_note-27)^ The Greek pottery found in [Eshera](/wiki/Eshera "Eshera"), further north along the coast, predates findings in the area of Sukhumi bay by a century suggesting that the centre of the original Greek settlement could have been there.^[\[27\]](#cite_note-braund-esheray-28)^{#cite_ref-braund-esheray_28-0}

It became busily engaged in the commerce between Greece and the indigenous tribes, importing salt^[\[28\]](#cite_note-braund-economy-29)^ and wares from many parts of Greece, and exporting local timber, [linen](/wiki/Linen "Linen"), and [hemp](/wiki/Hemp "Hemp"). It was also a prime center of [slave trade](/wiki/History_of_slavery "History of slavery") in Colchis.^[\[29\]](#cite_note-30)^ The city and its surroundings were remarkable for the multitude of languages spoken in its bazaars.^[\[30\]](#cite_note-31)^{#cite_ref-31}

Although the sea made serious inroads upon the territory of Dioscurias, it continued to flourish and became one of the key cities in the realm of [Mithridates VI of Pontus](/wiki/Mithridates_VI_of_Pontus "Mithridates VI of Pontus") in the 2nd century BC and supported his cause until the end. Dioscurias issued bronze coinage around 100 BC featuring the symbols of the [Dioskuri](/wiki/Dioskuri "Dioskuri") and [Dionysus](/wiki/Dionysus "Dionysus").^[\[31\]](#cite_note-braund-mithridates-32)^ Under the [Roman emperor](/wiki/Roman_emperor "Roman emperor") [Augustus](/wiki/Augustus "Augustus") the city assumed the name of *Sebastopolis* ([Greek](/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language"): Σεβαστούπολις).^[\[32\]](#cite_note-hewitt_taah-33)^ But its prosperity was past, and in the 1st century [Pliny the Elder](/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder "Pliny the Elder") described the place as virtually deserted though the town still continued to exist during the times of [Arrian](/wiki/Arrian "Arrian") in the 130s.^[\[33\]](#cite_note-34)^ The remains of towers and walls of Sebastopolis have been found underwater; on land the lowest levels so far reached by archaeologists are of the 1st and 2nd centuries AD. According to [Gregory of Nyssa](/wiki/Gregory_of_Nyssa "Gregory of Nyssa") there were Christians in the city in the late 4th century.^[\[34\]](#cite_note-35)^ In 542 the Romans evacuated the town and demolished its citadel to prevent it from being captured by [Sasanian Empire](/wiki/Sasanian_Empire "Sasanian Empire"). In 565, however, the emperor [Justinian I](/wiki/Justinian_I "Justinian I") restored the fort and Sebastopolis continued to remain one of the Byzantine strongholds in Colchis until [being sacked](/wiki/Marwan_ibn_Muhammad%27s_invasion_of_Georgia "Marwan ibn Muhammad's invasion of Georgia") by the [Arab](/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate") conqueror [Marwan II](/wiki/Marwan_II "Marwan II") in 736.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^{#cite_ref-35}

Afterwards, the town came to be known as *Tskhumi* .^[\[35\]](#cite_note-Adrian_Room-36)^ Restored by the [kings of Abkhazia](/wiki/Divan_of_the_Abkhazian_Kings "Divan of the Abkhazian Kings") from the Arab devastation, it particularly flourished during the [Georgian Golden Age](/wiki/Georgian_Golden_Age "Georgian Golden Age") in the 12th--13th centuries, when Tskhumi became a center of traffic with the European maritime powers, particularly with the [Republic of Genoa](/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa "Republic of Genoa"). The Genoese established their [trading port](/wiki/Gazaria_(Genoese_colonies)#Georgia "Gazaria (Genoese colonies)") in Tskhumi in the end of 13th century^[\[36\]](#cite_note-comai-37)^ and a Catholic bishopric existed there which is now a [titular see](/wiki/Titular_see "Titular see").^[\[37\]](#cite_note-38)^ A Genoese consulate was established in 1354 with the consul dispatched from [Caffa](/wiki/Caffa "Caffa"). In spite of occasional conflicts with the locals, the consulate functioned until 1456.^[\[36\]](#cite_note-comai-37)^ The city of Tskhumi became the summer residence of the [Georgian kings](/wiki/List_of_monarchs_of_Georgia#Kings_of_unified_Georgia_(1008–1490) "List of monarchs of Georgia"). According to Russian scholar V. Sizov, it became an important "cultural and administrative center of the [Georgian](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Georgia "Kingdom of Georgia") state.^[\[38\]](#cite_note-Giorgi_Sharvashidze2-39)^ A Later Tskhumi served as capital of the [Odishi](/wiki/Odishi "Odishi") --- [Megrelian](/wiki/Principality_of_Mingrelia "Principality of Mingrelia") rulers, it was in this city that [Vamek I](/wiki/Vameq_I_Dadiani "Vameq I Dadiani") ( 1384--1396), the most influential [Dadiani](/wiki/House_of_Dadiani "House of Dadiani"), minted his coins.^[\[38\]](#cite_note-Giorgi_Sharvashidze2-39)^{#cite_ref-Giorgi_Sharvashidze2_39-1}
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Gamba_-_view_of_sukhumi_fortress.JPG/220px-Gamba_-_view_of_sukhumi_fortress.JPG)](/wiki/File:Gamba_-_view_of_sukhumi_fortress.JPG) The Sohum-Kale fort in the early 19th century.

Documents of the 15th century clearly distinguished Tskhumi from [Principality of Abkhazia](/wiki/Principality_of_Abkhazia "Principality of Abkhazia").^[\[39\]](#cite_note-40)^ The [Ottoman](/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") navy occupied the town in 1451, but for a short time. Later contested between the princes of [Abkhazia](/wiki/Principality_of_Abkhazia "Principality of Abkhazia") and [Mingrelia](/wiki/Principality_of_Mingrelia "Principality of Mingrelia"), Tskhumi finally fell to the Turks in the 1570s. The new masters heavily fortified the town and called it *Sohumkale* , with *kale* meaning "fort" but the first part of the name of disputed origin. It may represent [Turkish](/wiki/Turkish_language "Turkish language") *su* ([Ottoman Turkish](/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish_language "Ottoman Turkish language"): صو), "water", and *kum* ([Ottoman Turkish](/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish_language "Ottoman Turkish language"): قوم), "sand", but is more likely to be an alteration of its earlier Georgian name.^[\[35\]](#cite_note-Adrian_Room-36)^{#cite_ref-Adrian_Room_36-1}
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Botanical_garden.Sukhum.jpg/220px-Botanical_garden.Sukhum.jpg)](/wiki/File:Botanical_garden.Sukhum.jpg) [Sukhumi Botanical Garden](/wiki/Sukhumi_Botanical_Garden "Sukhumi Botanical Garden")

At the request of the pro-Russian Abkhazian prince, the town was stormed by the [Russian Marines](/wiki/Naval_Infantry_(Russia) "Naval Infantry (Russia)") in 1810 and turned, subsequently, into a major outpost in the North West Caucasus. (See [Russian conquest of the Caucasus](/wiki/Russian_conquest_of_the_Caucasus#Black_Sea_Coast "Russian conquest of the Caucasus")). Sukhumi was declared the seaport in 1847 and was directly annexed to the [Russian Empire](/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire") after the ruling [Shervashidze](/wiki/House_of_Shervashidze "House of Shervashidze") princely dynasty was ousted by the Russian authorities in 1864. During the [Russo-Turkish War, 1877--1878](/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War_(1877%E2%80%931878) "Russo-Turkish War (1877–1878)"), the town was temporarily controlled by the Ottoman forces and Abkhaz-[Adyghe](/wiki/Adyghe_people "Adyghe people") rebels. After its annexation, Sukhumi became the administrative center of the [Sukhumi Okrug](/wiki/Sukhumi_Okrug "Sukhumi Okrug") of the [Kutais Governorate](/wiki/Kutaisi_Governorate "Kutaisi Governorate").^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/On_quay._Sukhum.jpg/220px-On_quay._Sukhum.jpg)](/wiki/File:On_quay._Sukhum.jpg) Sukhumi quay

Following the [Russian Revolution of 1917](/wiki/Russian_Revolution "Russian Revolution"), the town and Abkhazia in general were engulfed in the chaos of the [Russian Civil War](/wiki/Russian_Civil_War "Russian Civil War"). A short-lived [Bolshevik](/wiki/Bolsheviks "Bolsheviks") government was suppressed in May 1918 and Sukhumi was incorporated into the [Democratic Republic of Georgia](/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Georgia "Democratic Republic of Georgia") as a residence of the autonomous People's Council of Abkhazia and the headquarters of the Georgian governor-general. The [Red Army](/wiki/Red_Army "Red Army") and the local revolutionaries took the city from the Georgian forces on 4 March 1921, and declared Soviet rule. Sukhumi functioned as the capital of the "Union treaty" [Abkhaz Soviet Socialist Republic](/wiki/Socialist_Soviet_Republic_of_Abkhazia "Socialist Soviet Republic of Abkhazia") associated with the [Georgian SSR](/wiki/Georgian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic "Georgian Soviet Socialist Republic") from 1921 until 1931, when it became the capital of the Abkhazian Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic within the Georgian SSR. By 1989, Sukhumi had 120,000 inhabitants and was one of the most prosperous cities of Georgia. Many holiday [dachas](/wiki/Dacha "Dacha") for Soviet leaders were situated there.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Suhumi_Prokudin-Gorskii.jpg/220px-Suhumi_Prokudin-Gorskii.jpg)](/wiki/File:Suhumi_Prokudin-Gorskii.jpg) Sukhumi in 1912. Early color photo by [Sergei Prokudin-Gorskii](/wiki/Sergei_Prokudin-Gorskii "Sergei Prokudin-Gorskii")

Beginning with the [1989 riots](/wiki/1989_Sukhumi_riots "1989 Sukhumi riots"), Sukhumi was a centre of the [Georgian-Abkhaz conflict](/wiki/Abkhaz%E2%80%93Georgian_conflict "Abkhaz–Georgian conflict"), and the city was severely damaged during the [1992--1993 War](/wiki/War_in_Abkhazia_(1992%E2%80%931993) "War in Abkhazia (1992–1993)"). During the war, the city and its environs suffered almost daily air strikes and artillery shelling, with heavy civilian casualties.^[\[40\]](#cite_note-41)^ On 27 September 1993 the battle for Sukhumi was concluded by a full-scale campaign of [ethnic cleansing](/wiki/Ethnic_cleansing_of_Georgians_in_Abkhazia "Ethnic cleansing of Georgians in Abkhazia") against its majority Georgian population (see [Sukhumi Massacre](/wiki/Sukhumi_massacre "Sukhumi massacre")), including members of the pro-Georgian Abkhazian government ([Zhiuli Shartava](/wiki/Zhiuli_Shartava "Zhiuli Shartava"), [Raul Eshba](/wiki/Raul_Eshba "Raul Eshba") and others) and mayor of Sukhumi [Guram Gabiskiria](/wiki/Guram_Gabiskiria "Guram Gabiskiria"). Although the city has been relatively peaceful and partially rebuilt, it is still suffering the after-effects of the war, and it has not regained its earlier ethnic diversity. A relatively large infrastructure reconstruction program was launched in 2019--2020 focusing on the renovation of the waterfront, rebuilding city roads and cleaning city parks.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^ Its population in 2017 was 65,716, compared to about 120,000 in 1989. During summer holidays season its population usually doubles and triples with a large inflow of international tourists.^[\[41\]](#cite_note-censuses-42)^{#cite_ref-censuses_42-0}

In 2021, there was [unrest](/wiki/2021_Abkhazia_unrest "2021 Abkhazia unrest") in the city leading to the resignation of President [Aslan Bzhani](/wiki/Aslan_Bzhania "Aslan Bzhania").^[\[42\]](#cite_note-43)^{#cite_ref-43}

### Main sights {#Main_sights}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Sukhumi&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Main sights")\]

Sukhumi theatres which offer classical and modern performances, with the theatre season lasting from September to June. Several galleries and museums exhibit modern and historical Abkhaz visual art. [Sukhumi Botanical Garden](/wiki/Sukhumi_Botanical_Garden "Sukhumi Botanical Garden") was established in 1840 and is one of the oldest botanical gardens in the Caucasus.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Beslet_bridge.JPG/220px-Beslet_bridge.JPG)](/wiki/File:Beslet_bridge.JPG) Medieval [bridge](/wiki/Besleti_Bridge "Besleti Bridge") over the Besletka river known as the [Queen Tamar](/wiki/Tamar_of_Georgia "Tamar of Georgia") Bridge.

Sukhumi houses a number of historical monuments, notably the [Besleti Bridge](/wiki/Besleti_Bridge "Besleti Bridge") built during the reign of queen [Tamar of Georgia](/wiki/Tamar_of_Georgia "Tamar of Georgia") in the 12th century. It also retains visible vestiges of the defunct monuments, including the Roman walls, the medieval [Castle of Bagrat](/wiki/Bagrat%27s_Castle "Bagrat's Castle"), several towers of the [Kelasuri Wall](/wiki/Kelasuri_Wall "Kelasuri Wall"), also known as Great Abkhazian Wall, constructed between 1628 and 1653 by [Levan II Dadiani](/wiki/Levan_II_Dadiani "Levan II Dadiani") to protect his fiefdom from the Abkhaz tribes;^[\[45\]](#cite_note-46)^ the 14th-century Genoese fort and the 18th-century Ottoman fortress. The 11th century [Kamani Monastery](/wiki/Kamani_Monastery "Kamani Monastery") (12 kilometres (7 miles) from Sukhumi) is erected, according to tradition, over the tomb of Saint [John Chrysostom](/wiki/John_Chrysostom "John Chrysostom"). Some 22 km (14 mi) from Sukhumi lies [New Athos](/wiki/New_Athos "New Athos") with the ruins of the medieval city of [Anacopia](/wiki/Anacopia_Fortress "Anacopia Fortress"). The [Neo-Byzantine](/wiki/Byzantine_Revival_architecture "Byzantine Revival architecture") [New Athos Monastery](/wiki/New_Athos_Monastery "New Athos Monastery") was constructed here in the 1880s on behest of [Tsar](/wiki/Tsar "Tsar") [Alexander III of Russia](/wiki/Alexander_III_of_Russia "Alexander III of Russia").^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^{#cite_ref-46}

Northward in the mountains is the [Krubera Cave](/wiki/Krubera_Cave "Krubera Cave"), one of the deepest in the world, with a depth of 2,140 meters.^[\[46\]](#cite_note-47)^{#cite_ref-47}  

### Education {#Education}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Sukhumi&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Education")\]

The city hosts a number of research and educational institutions, including the [Abkhazian State University](/wiki/Abkhazian_State_University "Abkhazian State University"), the Sukhumi Open Institute and about half a dozen of vocational education colleges. From 1945 to 1954 the city's electron physics laboratory was involved in the Soviet program to develop nuclear weapons. Additionally, the [Abkhaz State Archive](/wiki/Abkhaz_State_Archive "Abkhaz State Archive") is located in the city.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^

Until 19th century young people from Abkhazia usually received their education mainly at religious schools (Muslims at Madrasas and Christians at Seminaries), although a small number of children from wealthy families had opportunity to travel to foreign countries for education.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^

The first modern educational institutions (both schools and colleges) were established in the late 19th-early 20th century and rapidly grew until the second half of the 20th century. For example, the number of college students grew from few dozens in the 1920s to several thousands in the 1980s.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^

According to the official statistical data, Abkhazia has 12 TVET colleges (as of 2019, est.) providing education and vocational training to youth mostly in the capital city, though there are several colleges in all major district centers. Independent international assessments suggest that these colleges train in about 20 different specialties attracting between 1200 and 1500 young people annually (aged between 16 and 29) (as of 2019, est.).^[\[47\]](#cite_note-ugsra.org-48)^ The largest colleges are as follows:^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^{#cite_ref-ugsra.org_48-0}

* Abkhaz Multiindustrial College (1959) (from 1959 to 1999 -- Sukhumi Trade and Culinary School),
* Sukhumi State College (1904) (from 1904 to 1921 -- Sukhumi Real School; from 1921 to 1999 -- Sukhumi Industrial Technical School),
* Sukhumi Art College (1934) (from 1934 to 1966 -- Sukhumi Art Studio). This college is also a home for a relatively large collection of local paintings and sculptures accumulated mainly during past 60 years.
* Sukhumi Medical College (1931)

Higher education in Sukhumi currently is represented by one university, [Abkhazian State University](/wiki/Abkhazian_State_University "Abkhazian State University"),^[\[48\]](#cite_note-49)^ which has a special status in the education system in Abkhazia and it manages its own budget.^[\[49\]](#cite_note-50)^{#cite_ref-50}

[Abkhaz State University](/wiki/Abkhazian_State_University "Abkhazian State University") (1979), has its own campus which is a home for 42 departments organized into 8 faculties providing education to about 3300 students (as of 2019, est.).^[\[47\]](#cite_note-ugsra.org-48)^{#cite_ref-ugsra.org_48-1}

Administration {#Administration}
--------------------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Sukhumi&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Administration")\]

On 2 February 2000, President Ardzinba dismissed temporary Mayor [Leonid Osia](/w/index.php?title=Leonid_Osia&action=edit&redlink=1 "Leonid Osia (page does not exist)") and appointed [Leonid Lolua](/w/index.php?title=Leonid_Lolua&action=edit&redlink=1 "Leonid Lolua (page does not exist)") in his stead.^[\[52\]](#cite_note-apress-agov-narod-53)^ Lolua was reappointed on 10 May 2001 following the March 2001 local elections.^[\[53\]](#cite_note-press2001-92-54)^{#cite_ref-press2001-92_54-0}

On 5 November 2004, in the heated aftermath of the [2004 presidential election](/wiki/Abkhazian_presidential_election,_2004 "Abkhazian presidential election, 2004"), president [Vladislav Ardzinba](/wiki/Vladislav_Ardzinba "Vladislav Ardzinba") appointed head of the [Gulripshi District](/wiki/Gulripshi_District "Gulripshi District") assembly [Adgur Kharazia](/wiki/Adgur_Kharazia "Adgur Kharazia") as acting mayor. During his first speech he called upon the two leading candidates, [Sergei Bagapsh](/wiki/Sergei_Bagapsh "Sergei Bagapsh") and [Raul Khadjimba](/wiki/Raul_Khadjimba "Raul Khadjimba"), to both withdraw.^[\[54\]](#cite_note-rferl041110-55)^{#cite_ref-rferl041110_55-0}

On 16 February 2005, after his election as president, Bagapsh replaced Kharazia with [Astamur Adleiba](/wiki/Astamur_Adleiba "Astamur Adleiba"), who had been Minister for Youth, Sports, Resorts and Tourism until December 2004.^[\[55\]](#cite_note-agov1498-56)^ In the 11 February 2007 local elections, Adleiba successfully defended his seat in the Sukhumi city assembly and was thereupon reappointed mayor by Bagapsh on 20 March.^[\[56\]](#cite_note-57)^{#cite_ref-57}

In April 2007, while President Bagapsh was in Moscow for medical treatment, the results of an investigation into corruption within the Sukhumi city administration were made public. The investigation found that large sums had been embezzled and upon his return, on 2 May, Bagapsh fired Adleiba along with his deputy [Boris Achba](/w/index.php?title=Boris_Achba&action=edit&redlink=1 "Boris Achba (page does not exist)"), the head of the Sukhumi's finance department [Konstantin Tuzhba](/w/index.php?title=Konstantin_Tuzhba&action=edit&redlink=1 "Konstantin Tuzhba (page does not exist)") and the head of the housing department [David Jinjolia](/w/index.php?title=David_Jinjolia&action=edit&redlink=1 "David Jinjolia (page does not exist)").^[\[57\]](#cite_note-iwpr335985-58)^ On 4 June Adleiba paid back to the municipal budget 200,000 rubels.^[\[58\]](#cite_note-59)^ and on 23 July, he resigned from the Sukhumi city council, citing health reasons and the need to travel abroad for medical treatment.^[\[59\]](#cite_note-60)^{#cite_ref-60}

On 15 May 2007, president Bagapsh released [Alias Labakhua](/wiki/Alias_Labakhua "Alias Labakhua") as First Deputy Chairman of the State Customs Committee and appointed him acting Mayor of Sukhumi, a post temporarily fulfilled by former Vice-Mayor [Anzor Kortua](/w/index.php?title=Anzor_Kortua&action=edit&redlink=1 "Anzor Kortua (page does not exist)"). On 27 May Labakhua appointed [Vadim Cherkezia](/w/index.php?title=Vadim_Cherkezia&action=edit&redlink=1 "Vadim Cherkezia (page does not exist)") as Deputy Chief of staff.^[\[60\]](#cite_note-uzel115078-61)^ On 2 September, Labakhua won the by-election in constituency No. 21, which had become necessary after Adleiba relinquished his seat. Adleiba was the only candidate and voter turnout was 34%, higher than the 25% required.^[\[61\]](#cite_note-62)^ Since Adleiba was now a member of the city assembly, president Bagapsh could permanently appoint him Mayor of Sukhumi on 18 September.^[\[62\]](#cite_note-63)^{#cite_ref-63}

Following the [May 2014 Revolution](/wiki/Abkhazian_Revolution "Abkhazian Revolution") and the election of [Raul Khajimba](/wiki/Raul_Khajimba "Raul Khajimba") as president, he on 22 October dismissed Labakhua and again appointed (as acting Mayor) [Adgur Kharazia](/wiki/Adgur_Kharazia "Adgur Kharazia"), who at that point was Vice Speaker of the [People's Assembly](/wiki/People%27s_Assembly_of_Abkhazia "People's Assembly of Abkhazia").^[\[63\]](#cite_note-apress13326-64)^ Kharazia won the 4 April 2015 by-election to the City Council in constituency no. 3 unopposed,^[\[64\]](#cite_note-alhra30-65)^ and was confirmed as mayor by Khajimba on 4 May.^[\[65\]](#cite_note-ukaz120-66)^ The [6th convocation of the Sukhumi City Council](/wiki/6th_convocation_of_the_Sukhumi_City_Council "6th convocation of the Sukhumi City Council") was elected 13 April 2016.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^{#cite_ref-ukaz120_66-0}

1. **[\^](#cite_ref-1)** The political status of Abkhazia is disputed. Having unilaterally declared independence from [Georgia](/wiki/Georgia_(country) "Georgia (country)") in 1992, Abkhazia [is formally recognised as an independent state](/wiki/International_recognition_of_Abkhazia_and_South_Ossetia "International recognition of Abkhazia and South Ossetia") by 5 UN member states (two other states previously recognised it but then withdrew their recognition), while the remainder of the international community recognizes it as *[de jure](/wiki/De_jure "De jure")* Georgian territory. Georgia continues to claim the area as its own territory, designating it as [Russian-occupied territory](/wiki/Occupied_territories_of_Georgia "Occupied territories of Georgia").{#cite_note-1}
