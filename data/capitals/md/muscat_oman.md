**Muscat** ([Arabic](/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language"): مَسْقَط, [romanized](/wiki/Romanization_of_Arabic "Romanization of Arabic"): *Masqaṭ*;
pronounced [\[ˈmasqatˤ\]](/wiki/Help:IPA/Arabic "Help:IPA/Arabic")) is the [capital](/wiki/Capital_(political) "Capital (political)") and most populous city in [Oman](/wiki/Oman "Oman"). It is the seat of the [Governorate of Muscat](/wiki/Muscat_(governorate) "Muscat (governorate)"). According to the [National Centre for Statistics and Information](/wiki/National_Centre_for_Statistics_and_Information "National Centre for Statistics and Information") (NCSI), the population of the Muscat Governorate in 2022 was 1.72 million.^[\[4\]](#cite_note-ncsi.gov.om-4)^ The metropolitan area includes six [provinces](/wiki/Provinces_of_Oman "Provinces of Oman"), called *[wilayats](/wiki/Wilayat "Wilayat")*, and spans approximately 3,500 km^2^ (1,400 sq mi),^[\[5\]](#cite_note-5)^ making it the largest city by area on the Arabian Peninsula.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^ Known since the early 1st century CE as a leading port for trade between the [west](/wiki/Western_world "Western world") and the [east](/wiki/Eastern_world "Eastern world"), Muscat was ruled successively by various indigenous tribes, as well as by foreign powers such as the [Persians](/wiki/Sassanid_Empire "Sassanid Empire"), the [Portuguese Empire](/wiki/Portuguese_Empire "Portuguese Empire") and the [Ottoman Empire](/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire"). In the 18th century, Muscat was a regional military power: its influence extended as far as [East Africa](/wiki/East_Africa "East Africa") and [Zanzibar](/wiki/Zanzibar "Zanzibar"). As an important port town in the [Gulf of Oman](/wiki/Gulf_of_Oman "Gulf of Oman"), Muscat attracted foreign traders and settlers such as the Persians, the [Balochs](/wiki/Baloch_people "Baloch people") and the [Sindhis](/wiki/Sindhis "Sindhis"). Beginning in 1970, after the accession of [Qaboos bin Said](/wiki/Qaboos_bin_Said "Qaboos bin Said") as the [Sultan](/wiki/Sultan "Sultan") of Oman, Muscat experienced rapid infrastructural development; it developed a vibrant economy and became a multi-ethnic society. The [Globalization and World Cities Research Network](/wiki/Globalization_and_World_Cities_Research_Network "Globalization and World Cities Research Network") classifies Muscat as a Beta-level [Global City](/wiki/Global_City "Global City").^[\[6\]](#cite_note-6)^{#cite_ref-6}

The [Hajar Mountains](/wiki/Hajar_Mountains "Hajar Mountains") dominate the view from Muscat. The city lies on the [Arabian Sea](/wiki/Arabian_Sea "Arabian Sea"), along the Gulf of Oman, near the strategically important [Straits of Hormuz](/wiki/Straits_of_Hormuz "Straits of Hormuz"). Low-lying white buildings are a typical feature of its architecture. The city's [port district](/wiki/Mina_Qaboos "Mina Qaboos") of [Muttrah](/wiki/Muttrah "Muttrah"), with its [corniche](/wiki/Corniche "Corniche") and harbour, are at the north-eastern edge of the city. Muscat's economy is dominated by trade, petroleum, liquified natural gas and porting.  

Toponymy {#Toponymy}
--------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Muscat&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Toponymy")\]

[Ptolemy](/wiki/Ptolemy "Ptolemy")'s *Map of Arabia* identifies the territories of *Cryptus Portus* ^[\[7\]](#cite_note-Foster_1844,_p.231-7)^ and *Moscha Portus* .^[\[8\]](#cite_note-8)^ Scholars are divided in opinion on which of the two is related to the city of *Muscat* . Similarly, [Arrianus](/wiki/Arrian "Arrian") references *Omana* and *Moscha* in *Voyage of Nearchus* . Interpretations of Arrianus' work by [William Vincent](/wiki/William_Vincent_(priest) "William Vincent (priest)") and [Jean Baptiste Bourguignon d'Anville](/wiki/Jean_Baptiste_Bourguignon_d%27Anville "Jean Baptiste Bourguignon d'Anville") conclude that *Omana* was a reference to *Oman* , while *Moscha* referred to *Muscat* .^[\[9\]](#cite_note-9)^ Similarly, other scholars identify [Pliny the Elder](/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder "Pliny the Elder")'s reference to *Amithoscuta* to be *Muscat* .^[\[7\]](#cite_note-Foster_1844,_p.231-7)^{#cite_ref-Foster_1844,_p.231_7-1}

The origin of the word *Muscat* is disputed. Some authors claim that the word has [Arabic](/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language") origins -- from *moscha* , meaning an *inflated hide* or *skin* .^[\[10\]](#cite_note-10)^ Other authors claim that the name *Muscat* means *[anchorage](/wiki/Anchorage_(shipping) "Anchorage (shipping)")* or *the place of "letting fall the anchor"* .^[\[11\]](#cite_note-11)^ Other derivations include *muscat* from [Old Persian](/wiki/Old_Persian "Old Persian"), meaning *strong-scented* ,^[\[12\]](#cite_note-12)^ or from [Arabic](/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language"), meaning *falling-place* ,^[\[13\]](#cite_note-13)^ or *hidden* .^[\[14\]](#cite_note-14)^ *Cryptus Portus* is synonymous with Oman ("hidden land"). But "Ov-man" (Omman), and the old Sumerian name *Magan (Maa-kan)* , means sea-people in Arabic. An inhabitant is a *Muscatter* , *Muscatian* , *Muscatite* or *Muscatan* . In 1793 AD the capital was transferred from [Rustaq](/wiki/Rustaq "Rustaq") to Muscat.{#cite_ref-14}  

History {#History}
------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Muscat&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: History")\]  
For a chronological guide, see [Timeline of Muscat](/wiki/Timeline_of_Muscat "Timeline of Muscat").

Evidence of communal activity in the area around Muscat dates back to the 6th millennium BC in Ras al-Hamra, where burial sites of fishermen have been found. The graves appear to be well formed and indicate the existence of burial rituals. South of Muscat, remnants of [Harappan](/wiki/Harappa "Harappa") pottery indicate some level of contact with the [Indus Valley civilisation](/wiki/Indus_Valley_civilisation "Indus Valley civilisation").^[\[15\]](#cite_note-15)^ Muscat's notability as a port was acknowledged as early as the 1st century AD by the [Greek](/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") geographer [Ptolemy](/wiki/Ptolemy "Ptolemy"), who referred to it as *Cryptus Portus* (the *Hidden Port* ), and by [Pliny the Elder](/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder "Pliny the Elder"), who called it *Amithoscuta* .^[\[16\]](#cite_note-16)^{#cite_ref-16}

The port fell to a [Sassanid](/wiki/Sassanid_Empire "Sassanid Empire") invasion in the 3rd century AD, under the rule of [Shapur I](/wiki/Shapur_I "Shapur I"),^[\[17\]](#cite_note-17)^ while conversion to Islam occurred during the 7th century. Muscat's importance as a trading port continued to grow in the centuries that followed, under the influence of the [Azd dynasty](/wiki/Azd "Azd"), a local tribe. The establishment of the First [Imamate](/wiki/Imamate "Imamate") in the 9th century was the first step in consolidating disparate Omani tribal factions under the banner of an [Ibadi](/wiki/Ibadi "Ibadi") state. However, tribal skirmishes continued, allowing the [Abbasids](/wiki/Abbasid "Abbasid") of [Baghdad](/wiki/Baghdad "Baghdad") to conquer Oman. The Abbasids occupied the region until the 11th century, when they were driven out by the local Yahmad tribe. Power over Oman shifted from the Yahmad tribe to the [Azdi](/wiki/Azd "Azd") Nabahinah clan, during whose rule, the people of coastal ports such as Muscat prospered from maritime trade and close alliances with the [Indian subcontinent](/wiki/Indian_subcontinent "Indian subcontinent"), at the cost of the alienation of the people of the interior of Oman. The [Portuguese](/wiki/Portuguese_empire "Portuguese empire") admiral [Afonso de Albuquerque](/wiki/Afonso_de_Albuquerque "Afonso de Albuquerque") sailed to Muscat in 1507, in an attempt to establish trade relations. As he approached the harbour, his ships were fired on. He then decided to conquer Muscat. Most of the city burned to the ground during and after the fighting.{#cite_ref-17}
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/MASCATE_Ant%C3%B3nio_Bocarro.png/250px-MASCATE_Ant%C3%B3nio_Bocarro.png)](/wiki/File:MASCATE_Ant%C3%B3nio_Bocarro.png) Muscat (Mascate) Portuguese Fortress in the 17th century. António Bocarro Book of Fortress.

The Portuguese maintained a hold on Muscat for over a century, despite challenges from [Persia](/wiki/Persia "Persia") and a bombardment of the town by the [Ottoman Turks](/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") in 1546.^[\[18\]](#cite_note-18)^ The Turks twice captured Muscat from the Portuguese, in the [Capture of Muscat (1552)](/wiki/Capture_of_Muscat_(1552) "Capture of Muscat (1552)") and 1581--88. The election of [Nasir bin Murshid Al-Ya'rubi](/wiki/Nasir_bin_Murshid "Nasir bin Murshid") as Imam of Oman in 1624 changed the balance of power again in the region, from the Persians and the Portuguese to local Omanis. Among the most important castles and forts in Muscat, the [Al Jalali Fort](/wiki/Al_Jalali_Fort "Al Jalali Fort") and the [Al-Mirani Fort](/wiki/Al-Mirani_Fort "Al-Mirani Fort") are the most prominent buildings left by the Portuguese. On August 16, 1648, the Imam dispatched an army to Muscat, which captured and demolished the high towers of the Portuguese, weakening their grip over the town. Decisively, in 1650, a small but determined body of the Imam's troops attacked the port at night, forcing an eventual Portuguese surrender on January 23, 1650.^[\[19\]](#cite_note-19)^ A civil war and repeated incursions by the Persian king [Nader Shah](/wiki/Nader_Shah "Nader Shah") in the 18th century destabilised the region, and further strained relations between the interior and Muscat. This power vacuum in Oman led to the emergence of the [Al Bu Sa'id dynasty](/wiki/Al_Bu_Sa%27id_Dynasty "Al Bu Sa'id Dynasty"), which has ruled Oman ever since.^[\[20\]](#cite_note-20)^
{#cite_ref-20}  
"Muscat is a large and very populous town, flanked on both sides with high mountains and the front is close to the water's edge; behind, towards the interior, there is a plain as large as the square of Lisbon, all covered with salt pans. \[T\]here are orchards, gardens, and palm groves with wells for watering them by means of swipes and other engines. The harbour is small, shaped like a horse-shoe and sheltered from every wind."  
---[Afonso de Albuquerque](/wiki/Afonso_de_Albuquerque "Afonso de Albuquerque"), after the fall of Muscat, in 1507.^[\[21\]](#cite_note-Miles_1997,_p.147-21)^

Muscat's naval and military supremacy was re-established in the 19th century by [Said bin Sultan](/wiki/Said_bin_Sultan "Said bin Sultan"), who signed a treaty with U.S. President [Andrew Jackson](/wiki/Andrew_Jackson "Andrew Jackson")'s representative [Edmund Roberts](/wiki/Edmund_Roberts_(diplomat) "Edmund Roberts (diplomat)") on September 21, 1833.^[\[22\]](#cite_note-22)^ Having gained control over [Zanzibar](/wiki/Zanzibar "Zanzibar"), in 1840 Said moved his capital to [Stone Town](/wiki/Stone_Town "Stone Town"), the ancient quarter of [Zanzibar City](/wiki/Zanzibar_City "Zanzibar City"); however, after his death in 1856, control over Zanzibar was lost when it became an independent sultanate under his sixth son, [Majid bin Said](/wiki/Majid_bin_Said "Majid bin Said") (1834/5--1870), while the third son, [Thuwaini bin Said](/wiki/Thuwaini_bin_Said "Thuwaini bin Said"), became the Sultan of Oman.{#cite_ref-22}
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/The_Persian_problem%3B_an_examination_of_the_rival_positions_of_Russia_and_Great_Britain_in_Persia%2C_with_some_account_of_the_Persian_gulf_and_the_Bagdad_railway_%281903%29_%2814577292147%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/370px-thumbnail.jpg)](/wiki/File:The_Persian_problem;_an_examination_of_the_rival_positions_of_Russia_and_Great_Britain_in_Persia,_with_some_account_of_the_Persian_gulf_and_the_Bagdad_railway_(1903)_(14577292147)_(cropped).jpg) A view of Muscat, ca. 1902

By the 19th century, a large Hindu merchant community in the port city dominated its commercial life. It is argued that their settlement at least since the fifteenth century; one of the reasons is that the Portuguese relied heavily on them to secure a trade monopoly in the Indian Ocean and Persian Gulf area. The [Sindhis](/wiki/Sindhis "Sindhis") were amongst the first during this era, followed by the [Kutchis](/wiki/Kutchi_people "Kutchi people"). The merchant community played an important role in expelling the Europeans in 1650. They were not affected by civil war that established the [Al Bu Sa'id dynasty](/wiki/Al_Bu_Sa%27id_Dynasty "Al Bu Sa'id Dynasty") and continued to prosper under [Ahmad bin Said al-Busaidi](/wiki/Ahmad_bin_Said_al-Busaidi "Ahmad bin Said al-Busaidi"). Important trade existed between the city and [Thatta](/wiki/Thatta "Thatta"), and later, [Kutch](/wiki/Kutch_district "Kutch district"), expanding to other parts of India.^[\[23\]](#cite_note-23)^{#cite_ref-23}
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Muscat_harbor.jpg/220px-Muscat_harbor.jpg)](/wiki/File:Muscat_harbor.jpg) Muscat harbour, ca. 1903. Visible in the background is [Fort Al Jalali](/wiki/Fort_Al_Jalali "Fort Al Jalali").

During the second half of the 19th century, the fortunes of the Al Bu Sa'id declined and friction with the Imams of the interior resurfaced. Muscat and [Muttrah](/wiki/Muttrah "Muttrah") were attacked by tribes from the interior in 1895 and again in 1915.^[\[24\]](#cite_note-24)^ A tentative ceasefire was brokered by the [British](/wiki/British_East_India "British East India"), which gave the interior more autonomy. However, conflicts among the disparate tribes of the interior, and with the Sultan of [Muscat and Oman](/wiki/Muscat_and_Oman "Muscat and Oman") continued into the 1950s, and eventually escalated into the [Dhofar Rebellion](/wiki/Dhofar_Rebellion "Dhofar Rebellion") (1962). The rebellion forced the Sultan [Said bin Taimur](/wiki/Said_bin_Taimur "Said bin Taimur") to seek the assistance of the British in quelling the uprisings from the interior. The failed assassination attempt of April 26, 1966 on Said bin Taimur led to the further isolation of the Sultan, who had moved his residence from Muscat to [Salalah](/wiki/Salalah "Salalah"), amidst the civilian armed conflict. On July 23, 1970, [Qaboos bin Said](/wiki/Qaboos_bin_Said "Qaboos bin Said"), son of the Sultan, staged a bloodless^[\[25\]](#cite_note-25)^ *[coup d'état](/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "Coup d'état")* in the Salalah palace with the assistance of the British, and took over as ruler.{#cite_ref-25}
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Muscat_between_years_1914_and_1918.jpg/220px-Muscat_between_years_1914_and_1918.jpg)](/wiki/File:Muscat_between_years_1914_and_1918.jpg) Muscat harbour during [World War I](/wiki/World_War_I "World War I")

With the assistance of the British, Qaboos bin Said put an end to the Dhofar uprising and consolidated disparate tribal territories. He renamed the country the *[Sultanate of Oman](/wiki/Sultanate_of_Oman "Sultanate of Oman")* (called *Muscat and Oman* hitherto), in an attempt to end to the interior's isolation from Muscat. Qaboos enlisted the services of capable Omanis to fill positions in his new government,^[\[26\]](#cite_note-26)^ drawing from such corporations as [Petroleum Development Oman](/wiki/Petroleum_Development_Oman "Petroleum Development Oman"). New ministries for social services such as health and education were established. The construction of [Mina Qaboos](/wiki/Mina_Qaboos "Mina Qaboos"), a new port conceived initially by Sa'id bin Taimur, was developed during the early days of Qaboos' rule. Similarly, a [new international airport](/wiki/Muscat_International_Airport "Muscat International Airport") was developed in Muscat's [Seeb](/wiki/Seeb "Seeb") district. A complex of offices, warehouses, shops and homes transformed the old village of [Ruwi](/wiki/Ruwi "Ruwi") in Muttrah into a commercial district.^[\[27\]](#cite_note-27)^ The first five-year development plan in 1976 emphasised infrastructural development of Muscat, which provided new opportunities for trade and tourism in the 1980s--1990s, attracting migrants from around the region. On June 6, 2007, [Cyclone Gonu](/wiki/Cyclone_Gonu "Cyclone Gonu") hit Muscat causing extensive damage to property, infrastructure and commercial activity.{#cite_ref-27}

Early photographs of the city and harbour, taken in the early 20th century by German explorer and photographer, [Hermann Burchardt](/wiki/Hermann_Burchardt "Hermann Burchardt"), are now held at the [Ethnological Museum of Berlin](/wiki/Ethnological_Museum_of_Berlin "Ethnological Museum of Berlin").^[\[28\]](#cite_note-28)^{#cite_ref-28}  

Geography and geology {#Geography_and_geology}
----------------------------------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Muscat&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Geography and geology")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Road_towards_Qantab%2C_Muscat.jpg/220px-Road_towards_Qantab%2C_Muscat.jpg)](/wiki/File:Road_towards_Qantab,_Muscat.jpg) Muscat's rugged terrain, with plutonic [Central Hajar Mountains](/wiki/Central_Hajar_Mountains "Central Hajar Mountains") dotting the landscape [![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Muscat_SPOT_1089.jpg/220px-Muscat_SPOT_1089.jpg)](/wiki/File:Muscat_SPOT_1089.jpg) Muscat by SPOT Satellite

Muscat is located in northeast Oman. The [Tropic of Cancer](/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer "Tropic of Cancer") passes south of the area. It is bordered to its west by the plains of the [Al Batinah Region](/wiki/Al_Batinah_Region "Al Batinah Region") and to its east by [Ash Sharqiyah Region](/wiki/Ash_Sharqiyah_Region_(Oman) "Ash Sharqiyah Region (Oman)"). The interior plains of [Ad Dakhiliyah Region](/wiki/Ad_Dakhiliyah_Region "Ad Dakhiliyah Region") border Muscat to the south, while the [Gulf of Oman](/wiki/Gulf_of_Oman "Gulf of Oman") forms the northern and western periphery of the city. The water along the coast of Muscat runs deep, forming two natural [harbours](/wiki/Harbour "Harbour"), in [Muttrah](/wiki/Muttrah "Muttrah") and Muscat. The [Central Hajar Mountains](/wiki/Central_Hajar_Mountains "Central Hajar Mountains")^[\[29\]](#cite_note-OmanTourism-29)^^[\[30\]](#cite_note-BradtOman2010-30)^ run through the northern coastline of the city.{#cite_ref-BradtOman2010_30-0}

[Volcanic rocks](/wiki/Volcanic_rock "Volcanic rock"), predominantly serpentinite and [diorite](/wiki/Diorite "Diorite") are apparent in the Muscat area and extend along the Gulf of Oman coast for ten or twelve 16 kilometres (9.9 mi) from the district of Darsait to Yiti.^[\[31\]](#cite_note-31)^ [Plutonic](/wiki/Pluton "Pluton") rocks constitute the hills and mountains of Muscat and span approximately 30 miles (48 km) from Darsait to Ras Jissah. These [igneous rocks](/wiki/Igneous_rock "Igneous rock") consists of [serpentinite](/wiki/Serpentinite "Serpentinite"), [greenstone](/wiki/Greenschist "Greenschist"), and [basalt](/wiki/Basalt "Basalt"), typical of rocks in southeastern regions of the [Arabian Peninsula](/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula "Arabian Peninsula"). South of Muscat, the volcanic rock strata are broken up and distorted, rising to a maximum height of 6,000 feet (1,800 m) in [Al-Dakhiliyah](/wiki/Ad_Dakhiliyah_Region "Ad Dakhiliyah Region"), a region which includes [Jebel Akhdar](/wiki/Jebel_Akhdar_(Oman) "Jebel Akhdar (Oman)"), the country's highest range. The hills in Muscat are mostly devoid of vegetation but are rich in [iron](/wiki/Iron "Iron").^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^{#cite_ref-31}

The [halophytic](/wiki/Halophytic "Halophytic") *[sabkha](/wiki/Sabkha "Sabkha")* type desert vegetation is predominant in Muscat.^[\[32\]](#cite_note-32)^ The [Qurum](/wiki/Qurum "Qurum") Nature Reserve contains plants such as the *Arthrocnemum Macrostachyum* and *[Halopeplis Perfoliata](/wiki/Halophyte "Halophyte")* . [Coral reefs](/wiki/Coral_reef "Coral reef") are common in Muscat. *[Acropora](/wiki/Acropora "Acropora")* reefs exist in the sheltered bays of the satellite towns of Jussah and Khairan.^[\[33\]](#cite_note-33)^ Additionally, smaller *[Porites](/wiki/Porites "Porites")* reef colonies exist in Khairan, which have fused to form a flat-top pavement that is visible at low tide. [Crabs](/wiki/Crab "Crab") and [spiny crayfish](/wiki/Crayfish "Crayfish") are found in the waters of the Muscat area, as are [sardines](/wiki/Sardine "Sardine") and [bonito](/wiki/Bonito "Bonito").^[\[34\]](#cite_note-34)^ [Glassfish](/wiki/Asiatic_glassfish "Asiatic glassfish") are common in freshwater [estuaries](/wiki/Estuary "Estuary"), such as the Qurum Nature Reserve.^[\[35\]](#cite_note-35)^{#cite_ref-35}

The Sultan Qaboos Street forms the main artery of Muscat, running west-to-east through the city. The street eventually becomes Al Nahdah Street near Al Wattayah. Several inter-city roads such as Nizwa Road and Al Amrat Road, intersect with Al Sultan Qaboos Road (in Rusail and [Ruwi](/wiki/Ruwi "Ruwi"), respectively). [Muttrah](/wiki/Muttrah "Muttrah"), with the Muscat Harbour, [Corniche](/wiki/Corniche "Corniche"), and [Mina Qaboos](/wiki/Mina_Qaboos "Mina Qaboos"), is located in the north-eastern coastline of the city, adjacent to the Gulf of Oman. Other coastal districts of Muscat include [Darsait](/wiki/Darsait "Darsait"), [Mina Al Fahal](/wiki/Mina_Al_Fahal "Mina Al Fahal"), Ras Al Hamar, [Al Qurum](/wiki/Al_Qurum "Al Qurum") Heights, Al Khuwair, and [Al Seeb](/wiki/Seeb,_Oman "Seeb, Oman"). Residential and commercial districts further inland include [Al Hamriyah](/wiki/Al_Hamriyah "Al Hamriyah"), [Al Wadi Al Kabir](/wiki/Wadi_Kabir "Wadi Kabir"), [Ruwi](/wiki/Ruwi "Ruwi"), Al Wattayah, [Madinat Qaboos](/wiki/Madinat_Qaboos "Madinat Qaboos"), Al Azaiba and [Al Ghubra](/wiki/Al_Ghubra "Al Ghubra").  

### Climate {#Climate}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Muscat&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Climate")\]  
Main article: [Climate of Muscat](/wiki/Climate_of_Muscat "Climate of Muscat")

Muscat features a hot, [arid climate](/wiki/Arid_climate "Arid climate") ([Köppen climate classification](/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification "Köppen climate classification") *BWh* ) with long, sweltering summers and warm winters. Annual [rainfall](/wiki/Rainfall "Rainfall") in Muscat is about 10 centimetres or 4 inches, falling mostly from December to April. In general, precipitation is scarce in Muscat, with the months May to November typically receiving only a trace of rainfall. However, in recent years, heavy precipitation events from tropical systems originating in the Arabian Sea have struck the city. [Cyclone Gonu](/wiki/Cyclone_Gonu "Cyclone Gonu") in June 2007 and [Cyclone Phet](/wiki/Cyclone_Phet "Cyclone Phet") in June 2010 affected the city with damaging winds and rainfall amounts exceeding 100 millimetres or 4 inches in just a single day. The climate generally is very hot and also very humid in the summer, with temperatures sometimes reaching as high as 45 °C or 113 °F.

Economy {#Economy}
------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Muscat&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Economy")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Land_Rover_and_Extreme_Sailing_Series%E2%84%A2_enjoy_thrill_of_Stadium_Racing_in_Muscat_%2813346080234%29.jpg/220px-Land_Rover_and_Extreme_Sailing_Series%E2%84%A2_enjoy_thrill_of_Stadium_Racing_in_Muscat_%2813346080234%29.jpg)](/wiki/File:Land_Rover_and_Extreme_Sailing_Series%E2%84%A2_enjoy_thrill_of_Stadium_Racing_in_Muscat_(13346080234).jpg) Offshore Sailing in Muscat

Muscat's economy, like that of Oman, is dominated by trade. The more traditional exports of the city included [dates](/wiki/Date_(fruit) "Date (fruit)"), [mother of pearl](/wiki/Mother_of_pearl "Mother of pearl"), and fish. Many of the [souks](/wiki/Souk "Souk") of Muttrah sell these items and traditional [Omani](/wiki/Omani "Omani") artefacts. [Petroleum Development Oman](/wiki/Petroleum_Development_Oman "Petroleum Development Oman") has been central to Muscat's economy since at least 1962 and is the country's second largest employer, after the government. Its major shareholders include [Shell](/wiki/Shell_plc "Shell plc"), Total, and Partex and its production is estimated to be about 720,000 barrels per day (114,000 m^3^/d). Muscat also has major trading companies such as the Suhail Bahwan Group, which is a trading partner for corporations such as [Toshiba](/wiki/Toshiba "Toshiba"), [Subaru](/wiki/Subaru "Subaru"), [Seiko](/wiki/Seiko "Seiko"), [Hewlett-Packard](/wiki/Hewlett-Packard "Hewlett-Packard"), [General Motors](/wiki/General_Motors_Corporation "General Motors Corporation"), [RAK Ceramics](/wiki/RAK_Ceramics "RAK Ceramics"); Saud Bahwan Group whose trading partners are [Toyota](/wiki/Toyota "Toyota"), [Daihatsu](/wiki/Daihatsu "Daihatsu"), KIA and [Hertz Rent-a-Car](/wiki/Hertz_Rent-a-Car "Hertz Rent-a-Car"); Zubair Automotive whose trading partners include [Mitsubishi](/wiki/Mitsubishi "Mitsubishi"), and [Chrysler](/wiki/Chrysler "Chrysler") brands such as Dodge; and [Moosa AbdulRahman Hassan](/wiki/Moosa_AbdulRahman_Hassan "Moosa AbdulRahman Hassan") which operates as one of the oldest automotive agencies in the entire region having been established in 1927.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^ The private Health Care sector of Muscat, Oman has numerous hospitals and clinics.

The [Muscat Securities Market](/wiki/Muscat_Securities_Market "Muscat Securities Market") is the principal stock exchange of Oman. It is located in Central Business District of Muscat and it was established in 1988, and has since distinguished itself as a pioneer among its regional peers in terms of transparency and disclosure regulations and requirements.^[\[39\]](#cite_note-39)^{#cite_ref-39}
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Ruwi_quarter_in_Mascat%2C_Oman.jpg/220px-Ruwi_quarter_in_Mascat%2C_Oman.jpg)](/wiki/File:Ruwi_quarter_in_Mascat,_Oman.jpg) Ruwi, the main business district of Muscat

Mina'a Sultan Qaboos, Muscat's main trading port, is a trading hub between the [Persian Gulf](/wiki/Persian_Gulf "Persian Gulf"), the [Indian subcontinent](/wiki/Indian_subcontinent "Indian subcontinent") and the Far East with an annual volume of about 1.6 million tons. However, the emergence of the [Jebel Ali Free Zone](/wiki/Jebel_Ali_Free_Zone "Jebel Ali Free Zone") in neighboring [Dubai](/wiki/Dubai "Dubai"), [United Arab Emirates](/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates "United Arab Emirates"), has made that port the premier maritime trading port of the region with about 44 million tons traded in cargo annually. Many infrastructural facilities are owned and operated by the government of Oman. [Omantel](/wiki/Omantel "Omantel") is the major telecommunications organization in Oman and provides local, long-distance and international dialing facilities and operates as the country's only [ISP](/wiki/Internet_Service_Provider "Internet Service Provider"). Recent liberalization of the mobile telephone market has seen the establishment of a second provider, [Ooredoo](/wiki/Ooredoo "Ooredoo").^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^

Muscat is home to multibillion-dollar conglomerate CK Industries with their headquarters located in [Ruwi](/wiki/Ruwi "Ruwi").^[\[40\]](#cite_note-amtek.ae-40)^ [Ajman](/wiki/Ajman "Ajman") based Amtek Industries also have a couple of offices around the city.^[\[40\]](#cite_note-amtek.ae-40)^ It is also home to Galfar Engineering,^[\[41\]](#cite_note-41)^ headed by P. Mohammed Ali.{#cite_ref-41}

The airline [Oman Air](/wiki/Oman_Air "Oman Air") has its head office on the grounds of [Muscat International Airport](/wiki/Muscat_International_Airport "Muscat International Airport").^[\[42\]](#cite_note-42)^{#cite_ref-42}  

Demographics {#Demographics}
----------------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Muscat&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Demographics")\]

According to the 2003 [census](/wiki/Census "Census") conducted by the Oman Ministry of National Economy, the population of Muscat is over 630,000, which included 370,000 males and 260,000 females.^[\[43\]](#cite_note-Oman_Census_2003,_p.6-43)^ Muscat formed the second largest governorate in the country, after [Al Batinah](/wiki/Al_Batinah_Region "Al Batinah Region"), accounting for 27% of the total population of Oman. As of 2003, [Omanis](/wiki/Omani "Omani") constituted 60% of the total population of Muscat, while expatriates accounted for about 40%.^[\[44\]](#cite_note-44)^ The [population density](/wiki/Population_density "Population density") of the city was 162.1 per km^2^.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^{#cite_ref-44}
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Shangri_La_resort_in_Muscat.jpg/218px-Shangri_La_resort_in_Muscat.jpg)](/wiki/File:Shangri_La_resort_in_Muscat.jpg) Shangri la in Muscat

The governorate of Muscat comprises six *[wilayats](/wiki/Wilayat "Wilayat")*: [Muttrah](/wiki/Muttrah "Muttrah"), Bawshar, [Seeb](/wiki/Seeb "Seeb"), Al Amrat, Muscat and [Qurayyat](/wiki/Qurayyat,_Oman "Qurayyat, Oman"). Seeb, located in the western section of the governorate, was the most populous (with over 220,000 residents), while Muttrah had the highest number of expatriates (with over 100,000).^[\[43\]](#cite_note-Oman_Census_2003,_p.6-43)^ Approximately 71% of the population was within the 15--64 age group, with the average Omani age being 23 years.^[\[45\]](#cite_note-45)^ About 10% of the population is [illiterate](/wiki/Literacy_rate "Literacy rate"), an improvement when compared to the 18% illiteracy rate recorded during the 1993 census. Expatriates accounted for over 60% of the [labour force](/wiki/Labour_force "Labour force"), dominated by males, who accounted for 80% of the city's total labour. A majority of expatriates (34%) was in engineering-related occupations, while most Omanis worked in engineering, clerical, scientific or technical fields. The [defense](/wiki/Royal_Army_of_Oman "Royal Army of Oman") sector was the largest employer for Omanis, while construction, [wholesale](/wiki/Wholesale "Wholesale") and [retail](/wiki/Retail "Retail") trade employed the largest number of expatriates.{#cite_ref-45}

The ethnic makeup of Muscat has historically been influenced by people not native to the Arabian Peninsula. [British Parliamentary](/wiki/British_Parliament "British Parliament") papers dating back to the 19th century indicate the presence of a significant [Hindu](/wiki/Hindu_people "Hindu people") [Gujarati](/wiki/Gujarat "Gujarat") [merchants](/wiki/Merchant "Merchant") in the city.^[\[46\]](#cite_note-46)^ Indeed, four [Hindu](/wiki/Hinduism "Hinduism") [temples](/wiki/Hindu_temple "Hindu temple") existed in Muscat ca. 1760.^[\[47\]](#cite_note-47)^ [Christianity](/wiki/Christianity "Christianity") flourished in Oman (Bēṯ Mazūnāyē "land of the Maganites"; a name deriving from its Sumerian designation) from the late 4th century to the 7th century. Missionary activity by the [Assyrians](/wiki/Assyrian_People "Assyrian People") of the [Church of the East](/wiki/Church_of_the_East "Church of the East") resulted in a significant Christian population living in the region, with a bishop being attested by 424 AD under the Metropolitan of Fars and Arabia. The rise of Islam saw the [Syriac](/wiki/Syriac_language "Syriac language") and Arabic-speaking Christian population eventually disappear. It is thought to have been brought back in by the [Portuguese](/wiki/Portuguese_Empire "Portuguese Empire") in 1507.^[\[48\]](#cite_note-48)^^\[*[unreliable source?](/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources "Wikipedia:Reliable sources")*\]^ [Protestant](/wiki/Protestant "Protestant") [missionaries](/wiki/Missionaries "Missionaries") established a hospital in Muscat in the 19th century.{#cite_ref-48}

Like the rest of Oman, [Arabic](/wiki/Arabic_(language) "Arabic (language)") is the predominant language of the city. In addition, [English](/wiki/English_(language) "English (language)"), [Balochi](/wiki/Balochi_language "Balochi language"), [Sindhi](/wiki/Sindhi_language "Sindhi language"), [Swahili](/wiki/Swahili_language "Swahili language") and [Indian](/wiki/India "India") languages such as [Bengali](/wiki/Bengali_language "Bengali language"), [Gujarati](/wiki/Gujarati_language "Gujarati language"), [Hindi](/wiki/Hindi "Hindi"), [Konkani](/wiki/Konkani_language "Konkani language"), [Malayalam](/wiki/Malayalam "Malayalam"), [Marathi](/wiki/Marathi_language "Marathi language"), [Odia](/wiki/Odia_language "Odia language"), [Tamil](/wiki/Tamil_language "Tamil language"), [Tulu](/wiki/Tulu_language "Tulu language"), [Telugu](/wiki/Telugu_language "Telugu language") and [Urdu](/wiki/Urdu "Urdu")^[\[49\]](#cite_note-49)^ are spoken by the residents of Muscat.{#cite_ref-49}

[Islam](/wiki/Islam "Islam") is the predominant religion in the city, with most followers being [Ibadi](/wiki/Ibadi "Ibadi") [Muslims](/wiki/Muslim "Muslim"). Non-Muslims are allowed to practise their religion, but may not [proselytize](/wiki/Proselytize "Proselytize") publicly or distribute religious literature. In 2017 the Sultanate of Oman unveiled the *Mushaf Muscat* , an [interactive](/wiki/Interactive_computing "Interactive computing") [calligraphic](/wiki/Calligraphy#Islamic_world "Calligraphy") Quran following a brief from the [Omani Ministry of Endowments and Religious Affairs](/wiki/Omani_Ministry_of_Endowments_and_Religious_Affairs "Omani Ministry of Endowments and Religious Affairs").^[\[50\]](#cite_note-50)^{#cite_ref-50}

Waterways
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Costa_Victoria_in_Port_Sultan_Qaboos%2C_Muscat%2C_Oman_20120408_1.jpg/220px-Costa_Victoria_in_Port_Sultan_Qaboos%2C_Muscat%2C_Oman_20120408_1.jpg)](/wiki/File:Costa_Victoria_in_Port_Sultan_Qaboos,_Muscat,_Oman_20120408_1.jpg) The Port Sultan Qaboos

Sultan Qaboos Port serves as one of the most important ports of [Muscat Governorate](/wiki/Muscat_Governorate "Muscat Governorate"), which is well known for being sailing of many commercial ships and boats. Here also, the traditional boats of Arabian Peninsula named [Dhows](/wiki/Dhow "Dhow") can be also seen. This port since many centuries have been a main commercial and financial centre in terms of its international maritime trade.


Airport

The main [airport](/wiki/Airport "Airport") is [Muscat International Airport](/wiki/Muscat_International_Airport "Muscat International Airport") (formerly known as Seeb International Airport) around 25 km (16 mi) from the city's business district of Ruwi and 15 to 20 km from the main residential localities of Al-Khuwair, Madinat Al Sultan Qaboos, Shati Al-Qurm and Al-Qurm. Muscat is the headquarters for the local [Oman Air](/wiki/Oman_Air "Oman Air"), which flies to several destinations within the [Middle East](/wiki/Middle_East "Middle East"), the Indian Subcontinent, East Africa and Europe.

Road transportation

The Muscat area is well serviced by paved roads and [dual-carriageway](/wiki/Dual-carriageway "Dual-carriageway") connects most major cities and towns in the country.
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Oman-Muscat-Muttrah-21-Marina.JPG/220px-Oman-Muscat-Muttrah-21-Marina.JPG)](/wiki/File:Oman-Muscat-Muttrah-21-Marina.JPG) Mutrah

Since November 2015, Public transportation in Muscat has been revamped with a bus network connecting most important parts of the city with a fleet of modern Mwasalat (earlier Oman National Transport Company buses. Mwasalat buses procured from VDL of the Netherlands and MAN of Germany have several hi-tech features, including free Wi-Fi.

* Route 1 (Ruwi-Mabela) serves people travelling major shopping destinations (Oman Avenues Mall, Muscat Grand Mall, Qurum City Centre, Muscat City Centre, Markaz al Bhaja) and Muscat Airport.
* Route 2 (Ruwi-Wadi Kabir) serves the residential and industrial district of [Wadi Kabir](/wiki/Wadi_Kabir "Wadi Kabir").
* Route 3 (Ruwi-Wadi Adei) serves the downmarket residential belt of Wadi Adei.
* Route 4 (Ruwi-Mattrah) serves the tourist destination of Muttrah Corniche, Al Alam Palace, Muttrah Fort, National Museum and Port Sultan Qaboos and churches/temples.
* Route 5 (Ruwi-Amerat) serves the rapidly developing Amerat suburb.
* Route 6 (Ruwi-SQU\&KOM) serves the student community of Sultan Qaboos University (SQU) and the office commuters of Knowledge Oasis Muscat (KOM).
* Route 7 serves the three major malls in Muscat -- Al Araimi Boulevard, Mall of Muscat and Markaz al Bhaja and Muscat City Centre.
* Route 8 serves Al Khuwair and Al Mouj Integrated Complex
* Route 9 serves Ansab and Misfah industrial area.
* Route 10 serves Seeb Souq and Mawelah Vegetable Market.
* Route 12 serves Oman Convention and Exhibition, Ghala areas.
* Route 14 serves Petroleum Development Oman, Qurm Natural Park, Qurm City Centre, Khoula Hospital.
* Routes 1b and 1A are special buses to Muscat International Airport.

Several forms of public transport are popular in Oman. Most popular are the "Baiza" buses, so named for the lower denomination of the [Omani rial](/wiki/Omani_rial "Omani rial"), the baiza (an adaptation of the [Indian](/wiki/India "India") lower denomination [paisa](/wiki/Paisa "Paisa")). These are relatively inexpensive and service all major roadways, as well as a wide and loose network of smaller byways in the greater Muscat metropolitan area, opportunistically dropping off and picking up passengers at any location. Less popular and slightly more expensive are large public buses, coloured red and green, whose service is limited to major roadways and point-to-point travel routes between Oman's major cities and towns. Taxis, also colour-coded orange and white, provide semi-personal transportation in the form of both individual hire and the same opportunistic roadway service as Baiza buses.

Baiza buses and colour-coded orange-and-white taxis are unmetered, after several government initiatives to introduce meters were rejected. The fare is set by way of [negotiation](/wiki/Negotiation "Negotiation"), although taxi drivers usually adhere to certain unwritten rules for fares within the city. In many countries, one is advised to negotiate a fare with the driver before getting into a taxi. However, in Oman, asking for the fare beforehand often demonstrates a passenger's newness and unfamiliarity with the area. One should always find out the normally accepted fare for one's journey from one's hotel or host before looking for a taxi. Taxis also generally take passengers to locations out of the city, including [Sohar](/wiki/Sohar "Sohar"), [Buraimi](/wiki/Buraimi "Buraimi") and [Dubai](/wiki/Dubai "Dubai").  

Culture {#Culture}
------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Muscat&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Culture")\]  
Main article: [Culture of Oman](/wiki/Culture_of_Oman "Culture of Oman")
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/DhowMuscat.jpg/220px-DhowMuscat.jpg)](/wiki/File:DhowMuscat.jpg) The traditional [Dhow](/wiki/Dhow "Dhow"), an enduring symbol of Oman^[\[53\]](#cite_note-coosoo-53)^

Outwardly, Oman shares many of the cultural characteristics of its Arab neighbours, particularly those in the [Gulf Cooperation Council](/wiki/Gulf_Cooperation_Council "Gulf Cooperation Council").^[\[54\]](#cite_note-kcom-54)^ Despite these similarities, important factors make Oman unique in the Middle East.^[\[54\]](#cite_note-kcom-54)^ These result as much from geography and history as from culture and economics.^[\[54\]](#cite_note-kcom-54)^ The relatively recent and artificial nature of the [state](/wiki/State_(polity) "State (polity)") of Oman makes it difficult to describe a national culture;^[\[54\]](#cite_note-kcom-54)^ however, sufficient cultural heterogeneity exists within its national boundaries to make Oman distinct from other Arab States of the Persian Gulf.^[\[54\]](#cite_note-kcom-54)^ Oman's cultural diversity is greater than that of its Arab neighbours, given its historical expansion to the Swahili Coast and the Indian Ocean.^[\[54\]](#cite_note-kcom-54)^{#cite_ref-kcom_54-5}

Oman has a long tradition of shipbuilding, as maritime travel played a major role in the Omanis' ability to stay in contact with the civilisations of the ancient world. [Sur](/wiki/Sur,_Oman "Sur, Oman") was one of the most famous shipbuilding cities of the Indian Ocean. The Al Ghanja ship takes one whole year to build. Other types of Omani ship include As Sunbouq and Al Badan.^[\[55\]](#cite_note-55)^{#cite_ref-55}
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Qasr_Al_Alam_Royal_Palace_%284%29.JPG/220px-Qasr_Al_Alam_Royal_Palace_%284%29.JPG)](/wiki/File:Qasr_Al_Alam_Royal_Palace_(4).JPG) Oman Council building in Muscat

In March 2016, archaeologists working off Al-Hallaniyah Island identified a shipwreck believed to be that of the *Esmeralda* from [Vasco da Gama](/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama "Vasco da Gama")'s 1502--1503 fleet. The wreck was initially discovered in 1998. Later underwater excavations took place between 2013 and 2015 through a partnership between the Oman Ministry of Heritage and Culture and [Blue Water Recoveries](/wiki/Blue_Water_Recoveries "Blue Water Recoveries") Ltd., a shipwreck recovery company. The vessel was identified through such artifacts as a "Portuguese coin minted for trade with India (one of only two coins of this type known to exist) and stone cannonballs engraved with what appear to be the initials of Vincente Sodré, da Gama's maternal uncle and the commander of the *Esmeralda* ".^[\[56\]](#cite_note-56)^{#cite_ref-56}
