**Douglas** ([Manx](/wiki/Manx_language "Manx language"): *Doolish* ,
pronounced [\[ˈdðuːlʲəʃ\]](/wiki/Help:IPA/Manx "Help:IPA/Manx")) is the [capital city](/wiki/Capital_(political) "Capital (political)") and largest settlement of the [Isle of Man](/wiki/Isle_of_Man "Isle of Man"), with a population of 26,677 (2021) and an area of 4.1 square miles (10.5 km^2^). It is located at the mouth of the [River Douglas](/wiki/River_Douglas,_Isle_of_Man "River Douglas, Isle of Man"), and on a sweeping bay of two miles (three kilometres). The River Douglas forms part of the [city's harbour](/wiki/Douglas_Harbour "Douglas Harbour") and main commercial port.

Douglas was a small settlement until it grew rapidly as a result of links with the English port of [Liverpool](/wiki/Liverpool "Liverpool") in the 18th century. Further population growth came in the following century, resulting during the 1860s in a staged transfer of the High Courts, the Lieutenant Governor's residence (actually located in nearby Onchan), and finally the seat of the legislature, [Tynwald](/wiki/Tynwald "Tynwald"), to Douglas^[\[1\]](#cite_note-1)^ from the ancient capital, [Castletown](/wiki/Castletown,_Isle_of_Man "Castletown, Isle of Man").{#cite_ref-1}

The city is the island's main hub for business, finance, legal services, shipping, transport, shopping, and entertainment. The annual [Isle of Man TT](/wiki/Isle_of_Man_TT "Isle of Man TT") motorcycle races start and finish in Douglas.  

Name {#Name}
------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Douglas,_Isle_of_Man&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Name")\]

It is generally agreed that the toponyms of English *Douglas* and [Manx](/wiki/Manx_language "Manx language") *Doolish* both come from [Proto-Celtic](/wiki/Proto-Celtic_language "Proto-Celtic language") \**duboglassio* -, where \**dubo* - (which survives today in [Welsh](/wiki/Welsh_language "Welsh language") as *du* and [Irish](/wiki/Irish_language "Irish language") as *dubh* ) meant 'black; deep' and \**glassio* - (surviving in Welsh *glais* and Irish *glais* ) meant 'water, river'; thus, it probably meant 'deep river'.^[\[2\]](#cite_note-2)^ The name of the city thus came from a nearby river. It is a common name for place names around the [British Isles](/wiki/British_Isles "British Isles"), related to other toponyms such as Dyfleis, [Dulais](/wiki/Dulais_Valley "Dulais Valley"), Develish, [Dawlish](/wiki/Dawlish "Dawlish"), [Dowlish](/wiki/Dowlish_Wake "Dowlish Wake") and, of course, other settlements named [Douglas](/wiki/Douglas_(disambiguation)#Places "Douglas (disambiguation)").{#cite_ref-2}  

History {#History}
------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Douglas,_Isle_of_Man&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: History")\]  

### Early history {#Early_history}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Douglas,_Isle_of_Man&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Early history")\]

In the absence of any [archaeological](/wiki/Archaeology "Archaeology") data, the origins of the city may be revealed by analysis of the original street and plot pattern. The discovery of a [bronze](/wiki/Bronze "Bronze") weapon in central Douglas,^[\[3\]](#cite_note-3)^ and the large Ballaquayle [Viking](/wiki/Viking "Viking") treasure hoard^[\[4\]](#cite_note-4)^ on the outskirts (now in the [Manx Museum](/wiki/Manx_Museum "Manx Museum") and [British Museum](/wiki/British_Museum "British Museum"), London^[\[5\]](#cite_note-5)^), both in the 1890s, hint at the early importance of the site now occupied by Douglas. Scholars agree that the name of the city derives from Early Celtic 'Duboglassio' meaning 'black river'.^[\[6\]](#cite_note-6)^ Douglas is twice referred to in the monastic *Chronicle of the Kings of Man and the Isles* : first in 1190, when the monks of [St Mary's Abbey at Rushen](/wiki/Rushen_Abbey "Rushen Abbey") were transferred there for a four-year stay; then again in 1313, when [Robert the Bruce](/wiki/Robert_the_Bruce "Robert the Bruce"), King of Scotland, spent the night at the "monastery of Duglas" on his way to seize [Castle Rushen](/wiki/Castle_Rushen "Castle Rushen").^[\[7\]](#cite_note-7)^ These may be references to the site of the later Nunnery, a little upstream from the port.{#cite_ref-7}

The first detailed documentation shows that in 1511^[\[8\]](#cite_note-8)^ there were only thirteen resident households in the settlement clustered north of the harbour; most of the property there was classified as "chambers"^[\[9\]](#cite_note-9)^ (unoccupied, unheated, single-celled structures) for which rent was paid by non-residents including clergy, officials and landowners from elsewhere on the island. This suggests that the city's nucleus originated as a non-urban port.^[\[10\]](#cite_note-10)^ Speculation links the store buildings with the Irish Sea herring fishery, and the import/export trade.{#cite_ref-10}  

### Growth and development {#Growth_and_development}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Douglas,_Isle_of_Man&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Growth and development")\]

In 1681 Thomas Denton described Douglas as "the place of greatest resort" on the Isle of Man, and by 1705 a clear picture of the early town emerges, with hints that its residential, market, and military defence functions were growing in importance^[\[11\]](#cite_note-11)^ alongside the port facility. The town thrived in the next 60 years, as imposing merchants' houses, large warehouses, quays and a pier were built to accommodate the burgeoning "running trade" ([smuggling](/wiki/Smuggling "Smuggling")): one of the stimuli for the town's expansion.^[\[12\]](#cite_note-12)^ Other forms of trade also grew, and after the [Revestment Act 1765](/wiki/Revestment_Act_1765 "Revestment Act 1765"), Douglas began to reap the benefits of transatlantic trade, due in part to co-operation at a local level with [Liverpool](/wiki/Liverpool "Liverpool").^[\[13\]](#cite_note-DouglasCen-13)^ Legitimate merchants who rose to prominence over the period included the Murreys, the Moores, and the Bacons.^[\[13\]](#cite_note-DouglasCen-13)^ The town's later prosperity was facilitated by the low cost of living, and the favourable legal status enjoyed by [English](/wiki/England "England") debtors and [half-pay](/wiki/Half-pay "Half-pay") officers.^[\[14\]](#cite_note-14)^ The initial growth and development of the ALR owed much to its natural harbour (now the Inner Harbour), since greatly expanded and improved. Over the 18th century, the town's population rose from about 800 in 1710 to nearly 2,500 in 1784.{#cite_ref-14}

Throughout the 19th century, the town's demographics followed the same trends as the United Kingdom, due to the [Industrial Revolution](/wiki/Industrial_Revolution "Industrial Revolution"). The number of holiday visitors grew from the early 19th century,^[\[13\]](#cite_note-DouglasCen-13)^ and from around 1870 onwards, the town was transformed into a leading holiday resort. But there were increasingly unsanitary conditions, and poor quality housing; again reflecting a trend seen across the United Kingdom.^[\[15\]](#cite_note-15)^ The open [sewage](/wiki/Sewage "Sewage"), [middens](/wiki/Midden "Midden") (domestic waste dumps), and smell from the harbour at low tide all contributed to the town's uncleanliness. Oil and gas lamps first appeared in the late 1820s and 1830s, the first hospital to join the [Dispensary](/wiki/Dispensary "Dispensary") was built in 1850, and in 1832 the scenic [Tower of Refuge](/wiki/St_Mary%27s_Isle_(Conister_Rocks_or_Tower_of_Refuge) "St Mary's Isle (Conister Rocks or Tower of Refuge)") was built in Douglas Bay to offer shelter and provisions for sailors awaiting rescue.^[\[16\]](#cite_note-16)^ Douglas in the first half of the 19th century often suffered from the destitution of its population and the many epidemics, in particular [cholera](/wiki/Cholera "Cholera").^[\[13\]](#cite_note-DouglasCen-13)^{#cite_ref-DouglasCen_13-3}

The rise of Douglas as the social and economic stronghold was recognised in 1869, when it became the home of the island's parliament, [Tynwald](/wiki/Tynwald "Tynwald"), and therefore the capital, an honour previously held by [Castletown](/wiki/Castletown,_Isle_of_Man "Castletown, Isle of Man"), a smaller town in the south of the island. Douglas's political landscape also changed significantly in the 19th century, in spite of the conservatism of some townsfolk: in 1844, for example, at a public meeting, the idea of a [town council](/wiki/Town_council "Town council") was rejected in favour of retaining the system of Town High Bailiffs; when the Town Bill Act was passed at Tynwald in 1852, the people of Douglas again rejected the idea. However, an Act passed later that decade, which did not include opt-out clauses, was accepted, and in 1860, Douglas elected its first town council, which was predominantly [middle class](/wiki/Middle_class "Middle class") in its makeup. The Town Commissioners could tackle the town's problems with greater efficiency, and by 1869 the sewage problem had been largely resolved.^[\[13\]](#cite_note-DouglasCen-13)^{#cite_ref-DouglasCen_13-4}

The Commissioners also worked to alter the anachronistic architecture of Douglas, built during the era of fishing and trading, and no longer amenable or safe for tourists. The proportion of the total Manx population living in Douglas was also expanding, with 35% living there by 1891. The [Victorian](/wiki/Victorian_era "Victorian era") and later modernisation of the town was achieved at the expense of the original maze-like layout of the oldest streets. These were cleared away in the new street schemes and slum clearances of the 1870s to 1920s. The town's infrastructure was radically altered for tourists' convenience, and in 1878 the Loch Promenade was constructed. In 1870, there were 60,000 visitors annually; by 1884, this had grown to 182,000. In 1887, 310,916 visited for [Queen Victoria's Golden Jubilee](/wiki/Queen_Victoria%27s_Golden_Jubilee "Queen Victoria's Golden Jubilee").^[\[13\]](#cite_note-DouglasCen-13)^{#cite_ref-DouglasCen_13-5}  

### Seafaring links {#Seafaring_links}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Douglas,_Isle_of_Man&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Seafaring links")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Douglas_lifeboat_station.jpg/220px-Douglas_lifeboat_station.jpg)](/wiki/File:Douglas_lifeboat_station.jpg) Douglas Lifeboat Station

The earliest organised ferry services emerged between Douglas and Whitehaven, established by William Nicholson in the 1750s.^[\[17\]](#cite_note-17)^ More formally, Nicholson's Packet Service began in 1765 or 1766, and the Post Office mail contract was secured by 1782. Sailings were irregular and heavily dependent on weather conditions. Steamer services brought a considerable improvement; the first regular link (en route between Greenock and Liverpool) was available from 1819.^[\[18\]](#cite_note-18)^ The formation of what became the [Isle of Man Steam Packet Company](/wiki/Isle_of_Man_Steam_Packet_Company "Isle of Man Steam Packet Company") in 1830 led to greatly improved services, and also laid the foundations for growth in both cargo and tourist traffic.^[\[19\]](#cite_note-19)^{#cite_ref-19}

The [Royal National Lifeboat Institution](/wiki/Royal_National_Lifeboat_Institution "Royal National Lifeboat Institution") (RNLI) has a [lifeboat](/wiki/Lifeboat_(rescue) "Lifeboat (rescue)") station at Douglas. The first lifeboat station in the port was established in 1802. In 1825 it was one of the first lifeboat stations to be provided with a purpose-built lifeboat; this was at the request of Sir [William Hillary](/wiki/William_Hillary "William Hillary"), the founder of the Institution. It was not operational between 1851 and 1868, and it closed in 1895. The station, established in 1874, operates the [Tyne-class lifeboat](/wiki/Tyne-class_lifeboat "Tyne-class lifeboat") *Sir William Hillary* . There are three memorials to Sir William in Douglas: one in St George's Church where he was buried in 1847, one on Loch Promenade, and the other on Douglas Head near where he established the [Tower of Refuge](/wiki/Tower_of_Refuge "Tower of Refuge") in 1832.^[\[20\]](#cite_note-20)^{#cite_ref-20}  

### Recent history {#Recent_history}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Douglas,_Isle_of_Man&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Recent history")\]

During the [First](/wiki/First_World_War "First World War") and [Second World Wars](/wiki/Second_World_War "Second World War"), some parts of Douglas and elsewhere in the Isle of Man were home to [internment camps](/wiki/Internment "Internment") for "[enemy aliens](/wiki/Enemy_alien "Enemy alien")". Douglas held seven out of the ten internment camps on the island during World War II, including: [Hutchinson](/wiki/Hutchinson_Internment_Camp "Hutchinson Internment Camp"), [Metropole](/wiki/Metropole_Internment_Camp "Metropole Internment Camp"), Central, Palace, Granville and [Sefton Camp](/wiki/Sefton_Internment_Camp "Sefton Internment Camp"). Sections of the Promenade were cordoned off and many guest houses were used for the purpose. The war dead of the island are commemorated on the [Douglas War Memorial](/wiki/Douglas_War_Memorial "Douglas War Memorial").

Douglas is now the main home of the island's offshore financial services industry, and also of the main shopping centre.

The city was the birthplace of brothers [Maurice](/wiki/Maurice_Gibb "Maurice Gibb"), [Robin](/wiki/Robin_Gibb "Robin Gibb") and [Barry](/wiki/Barry_Gibb "Barry Gibb") Gibb, members of the [Bee Gees](/wiki/Bee_Gees "Bee Gees").

Douglas was home to the [Summerland](/wiki/Summerland,_Isle_of_Man "Summerland, Isle of Man") [leisure centre](/wiki/Leisure_centre "Leisure centre"), which suffered a catastrophic fire in 1973.

In 2011 Douglas hosted the [Commonwealth Youth Games](/wiki/2011_Commonwealth_Youth_Games "2011 Commonwealth Youth Games").

On 20 May 2022, it was announced that, as part of the [Platinum Jubilee Civic Honours](/wiki/Platinum_Jubilee_Civic_Honours "Platinum Jubilee Civic Honours"), Douglas would receive [city status](/wiki/City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom "City status in the United Kingdom") by [Letters Patent](/wiki/Letters_Patent_(United_Kingdom) "Letters Patent (United Kingdom)") sometime later in 2022.^[\[21\]](#cite_note-21)^^[\[22\]](#cite_note-22)^ The letters patent was expected to be effective from 1 November 2022,^[\[23\]](#cite_note-:0-23)^^[\[24\]](#cite_note-24)^ although there were some reports in November 2022 that it was somehow delayed following the [death of Queen Elizabeth II](/wiki/Death_of_Queen_Elizabeth_II "Death of Queen Elizabeth II"), to a unknown date.^[\[25\]](#cite_note-City_delay-25)^^[\[26\]](#cite_note-26)^ A council meeting was said to discuss the issue in February 2023,^[\[27\]](#cite_note-27)^ but it was not discussed.^[\[28\]](#cite_note-28)^ [Queen Camilla](/wiki/Queen_Camilla "Queen Camilla") presented Letters Patent conferring city status on 20 March 2024.^[\[29\]](#cite_note-29)^{#cite_ref-29}  

Geography {#Geography}
----------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Douglas,_Isle_of_Man&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Geography")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Cmglee_Douglas_Isle_of_Man_aerial_view.jpg/220px-Cmglee_Douglas_Isle_of_Man_aerial_view.jpg)](/wiki/File:Cmglee_Douglas_Isle_of_Man_aerial_view.jpg) Aerial view of Douglas and the southern half of the Isle of Man

Douglas is on the east of the island near the [confluence](/wiki/Confluence "Confluence") of the two rivers [Dhoo](/wiki/River_Dhoo "River Dhoo") and [Glass](/wiki/River_Glass,_Isle_of_Man "River Glass, Isle of Man") forming the [Douglas](/wiki/River_Douglas,_Isle_of_Man "River Douglas, Isle of Man"). At Douglas, the river flows through the [quay](/wiki/Quay "Quay") and into [Douglas Bay](/wiki/Douglas_Harbour "Douglas Harbour"). A gently sloping valley runs inland and there are hills around the city. The city is surrounded by several villages, most notably [Onchan](/wiki/Onchan "Onchan") to the north-east (which forms a [conurbation](/wiki/Conurbation "Conurbation") with Douglas) and [Union Mills](/wiki/Union_Mills "Union Mills") to the west.  

### Climate {#Climate}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Douglas,_Isle_of_Man&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Climate")\]

The weather reporting station closest to Douglas is the coastal weather station at [Ronaldsway](/wiki/Ronaldsway "Ronaldsway"), about 7 miles (11 km) south-west of Douglas. The highest temperature recorded in Ronaldsway stands at 28.9 °C (84.0 °F),^[\[30\]](#cite_note-30)^ recorded in July 1983. On average, a temperature of 25 °C (77 °F) or more is recorded 0.8 days per year^[\[31\]](#cite_note-31)^ at Ronaldsway (1981--2010) and on average the highest temperature of the year is 24.1 °C (75.4 °F).^[\[32\]](#cite_note-32)^{#cite_ref-32}

On average, 14.5 days of air frost per year were recorded at Ronaldsway^[\[33\]](#cite_note-33)^ in the period 1981--2010. The lowest temperature recorded in Ronaldsway is −9.1 °C (15.6 °F),^[\[34\]](#cite_note-34)^ recorded in December 1961. For the period of 1981--2010, the lowest temperature of the year was −3.4 °C (25.9 °F) on average.^[\[35\]](#cite_note-35)^{#cite_ref-35}  

Demography {#Demography}
------------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Douglas,_Isle_of_Man&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Demography")\]

The Isle of Man census 2021 lists the population of Douglas as 26,677, a reduction from the 2011 population of 27,938. The 2001 census gave a population of 25,347. The city has 31.73% of the island's total population of 84,069.  

Governance {#Governance}
------------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Douglas,_Isle_of_Man&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Governance")\]

Douglas elects eight members of the House of Keys: two each from four constituencies (Central, East, South and North). The House of Keys is the lower house of [Tynwald](/wiki/Tynwald "Tynwald"), the Manx parliament. The local authority responsible for the running of services is [Douglas City Council](/wiki/Douglas_City_Council "Douglas City Council") (formerly Douglas Borough Council), which has 12^[\[36\]](#cite_note-36)^ elected councillors.{#cite_ref-36}  

Transport {#Transport}
----------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Douglas,_Isle_of_Man&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Transport")\]

Douglas is the hub of the island's bus network, with frequent services to Port Erin, Peel and Ramsey, as well as various more local bus services. The city also has the termini of the two main railway lines on the island: the [steam railway](/wiki/Isle_of_Man_Railway "Isle of Man Railway") to Port Erin and the [electric tramway](/wiki/Manx_Electric_Railway "Manx Electric Railway") to Ramsey. It is also the home of the [ferry terminal](/wiki/Isle_of_Man_Sea_Terminal "Isle of Man Sea Terminal"), from where the [Isle of Man Steam Packet Company](/wiki/Isle_of_Man_Steam_Packet_Company "Isle of Man Steam Packet Company") operates regular services to [Heysham](/wiki/Heysham "Heysham") and to Liverpool (Birkenhead in winter) and occasional services to Dublin and to Belfast.

Education {#Education}
----------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Douglas,_Isle_of_Man&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Education")\]

References to education in Douglas begin in the late 17th century, with mention of the schoolmastership, usually combined with the Chaplaincy of Douglas, and reference to a "petty school" in 1675.^[\[39\]](#cite_note-39)^ William Walker (1679--1729) was appointed to the mastership in 1700;^[\[40\]](#cite_note-40)^ school was probably kept in the old Chapel, in later Heywood Place.)^[\[41\]](#cite_note-41)^ Walker was later [preferred](/wiki/Clergy "Clergy") as Rector of Ballaugh, and received an honorary Doctorate. He is now remembered in his mother's Manx Gaelic lament on her sons: "Illiam Walker *as* Robin Tear".^[\[42\]](#cite_note-42)^{#cite_ref-42}
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Man_douglas_01.JPG/220px-Man_douglas_01.JPG)](/wiki/File:Man_douglas_01.JPG) Seafront, Douglas

The more formal history of education in the city begins with Bishop Thomas Wilson's establishment of the Douglas Grammar School. Alderman William Dixon (a native of Douglas, but resident in Dublin since at least the 1680s^[\[43\]](#cite_note-43)^) agreed to hand over his parents' former home, and passed over the New Bond Street property in 1714. The Bishop raised an endowment of £500 (Irish currency), half being funded by the Barrow Trustees, most of the balance being provided by the Bishop's Douglas relatives, the Murrey family.^[\[44\]](#cite_note-44)^ Even after this, the mastership of Douglas Grammar School continued to be combined with the Chaplaincy, now of the new Chapel of St Matthew. Details of the building of the original Grammar School are sparse, but it seems that William Murrey was deeply involved, retaining the use of the capacious storage vault under the school in lieu of repayment of £150 costs.^[\[45\]](#cite_note-45)^{#cite_ref-45}

Another notable Master of the Grammar School was Rev. Philip Moore (1705--1783) who was a native of the city, his father Robert Moore having been one of the "Undertakers" who organised the construction of St Matthews,^[\[46\]](#cite_note-46)^ and his mother Miss Katherine Kelly. Moore was appointed Master in 1735, and was reckoned to be a good classical scholar, and have "an inherent love of teaching".^[\[47\]](#cite_note-47)^{#cite_ref-47}

Today, Douglas has two high schools: [Ballakermeen High School](/wiki/Ballakermeen_High_School "Ballakermeen High School") and [St. Ninian's High School](/wiki/St_Ninian%27s_High_School,_Douglas "St Ninian's High School, Douglas"), and numerous primary schools. [University College Isle of Man](/wiki/University_College_Isle_of_Man "University College Isle of Man") and the [International Business School](/wiki/Isle_of_Man_International_Business_School "Isle of Man International Business School") are also located in Douglas.  

Landmarks {#Landmarks}
----------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Douglas,_Isle_of_Man&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Landmarks")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Douglas-IOM-horse-tram2.jpg/220px-Douglas-IOM-horse-tram2.jpg)](/wiki/File:Douglas-IOM-horse-tram2.jpg) A [horse tram](/wiki/Horsecar "Horsecar") passes a pedestrian crossing on Douglas promenade [![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/DouglasIsleofMan.jpg/220px-DouglasIsleofMan.jpg)](/wiki/File:DouglasIsleofMan.jpg) Douglas during the 1992 TT Races

Douglas has a number of attractions and items of interest:

* The [Tower of Refuge](/wiki/St_Mary%27s_Isle_(Conister_Rocks_or_Tower_of_Refuge) "St Mary's Isle (Conister Rocks or Tower of Refuge)") is a small castle-like shelter built upon Conister Rock in Douglas Bay as a sanctuary for shipwrecked sailors. Construction was instigated by Sir [William Hillary](/wiki/William_Hillary "William Hillary"), founder of the [RNLI](/wiki/RNLI "RNLI").
* [Douglas Head](/wiki/Douglas_Head "Douglas Head") is home to the [Grand Union Camera Obscura](/wiki/Grand_Union_Camera_Obscura "Grand Union Camera Obscura") which has recently undergone restoration and is open to the public during the summer months. Other artefacts and remnants of Victorian Tourism can still be found on walks around the area.
* The [horse-drawn trams](/wiki/Douglas_Horse_Tram "Douglas Horse Tram") that run along the promenade from the [Villa Marina](/wiki/Villa_Marina,_Isle_of_Man "Villa Marina, Isle of Man") to the [Manx Electric Railway](/wiki/Manx_Electric_Railway "Manx Electric Railway") station from spring to early autumn.
* [Steam trains](/wiki/Isle_of_Man_Steam_Railway "Isle of Man Steam Railway") run 15 miles from [Douglas railway station](/wiki/Douglas_railway_station "Douglas railway station") to [Port Erin](/wiki/Port_Erin "Port Erin") in the south of the island.
* The Grandstand on Glencrutchery Road marks the start and finish of the annual [TT Races](/wiki/Isle_of_Man_TT "Isle of Man TT") and various other motorsports.
* The [Gaiety Theatre](/wiki/Gaiety_Theatre,_Isle_of_Man "Gaiety Theatre, Isle of Man") and the Villa Marina are popular venues for all manner of stage acts: from rock music to comedy to drama to ballet. The Gaiety Theatre is one of the best surviving examples of the work of [Frank Matcham](/wiki/Frank_Matcham "Frank Matcham") and dates from 1900. Both venues have recently^\[*[when?](/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items "Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers")*\]^ undergone extensive renovations.
* The award-winning [Manx Museum](/wiki/Manx_National_Heritage "Manx National Heritage") in Kingswood Grove contains many of the most important cultural artefacts relating to the Manx nation. Highlights include the Calf of Man Crucifixion Stone, the Pagan Lady's necklace from the Viking excavations at Peel Castle, and the largest collection of [Archibald Knox](/wiki/Archibald_Knox_(designer) "Archibald Knox (designer)") materials. It also houses the National Art Collection, and the National Archives.
* The [Jubilee clock](/wiki/Jubilee_clock "Jubilee clock") is a street clock built in 1887 to mark the [Golden Jubilee](/wiki/Golden_Jubilee "Golden Jubilee") of [Queen Victoria](/wiki/Victoria_of_the_United_Kingdom "Victoria of the United Kingdom")'s reign. It is located at the foot of Victoria Street at its junction with Loch Promenade. The location also marked the lower terminus of the [Upper Douglas Cable Tramway](/wiki/Upper_Douglas_Cable_Tramway "Upper Douglas Cable Tramway").
* Other buildings of interest include Isola restaurant, in John Street, and the Douglas Hotel, on the North Quay, both merchants' houses from the mid-18th century; the [Castle Mona](/wiki/Castle_Mona "Castle Mona") (formerly the Quality Hotel), a seaside mansion built by [John Murray, 4th Duke of Atholl](/wiki/John_Murray,_4th_Duke_of_Atholl "John Murray, 4th Duke of Atholl") in 1804, awaiting refurbishment; and the Loch Promenade, a curving terrace of former boarding houses dating from the 1870s.

Douglas saw the first architectural essays of the Arts and Crafts architect [Baillie Scott](/wiki/Baillie_Scott "Baillie Scott").

* The breakwater extension was built outside the existing smaller one, which carried a [rail-mounted crane](/wiki/Breakwater_Crane_Railway "Breakwater Crane Railway"). It was completed in 1983 and opened by [Princess Alexandra](/wiki/Princess_Alexandra,_The_Honourable_Lady_Ogilvy "Princess Alexandra, The Honourable Lady Ogilvy").
* The Sunken Gardens on Loch Promenade were created as a result of the widening of the promenade at the turn of the 20th century. A construction line was installed for this task.

Sport {#Sport}
--------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Douglas,_Isle_of_Man&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Sport")\]

Douglas is the start and finish point for the [Isle of Man TT](/wiki/Isle_of_Man_TT "Isle of Man TT"). The race had been a part of the [FIM](/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_Motocyclisme "Fédération Internationale de Motocyclisme") [Motorcycle Grand Prix World Championship](/wiki/Grand_Prix_motorcycle_racing "Grand Prix motorcycle racing") but lost its status when a new British motorcycle Grand Prix was established in 1977.

The Isle of Man Cricket Club was formed in 1930 and later reformed as the IoM Cricket Association. It was elected a member of the International Cricket Council in 2004.^[\[48\]](#cite_note-48)^{#cite_ref-48}

The [National Sports Centre](/wiki/National_Sports_Centre_(Isle_of_Man) "National Sports Centre (Isle of Man)") (NSC), is a large multi-sports centre and [athletics](/wiki/Athletics_(sport) "Athletics (sport)") stadium located on Pulrose Road. Owned by the [Department of Tourism and Leisure](/wiki/Department_of_Tourism_and_Leisure "Department of Tourism and Leisure") it is operated by Manx Sport \& Recreation. [The King George V Bowl](/wiki/The_Bowl_(Douglas) "The Bowl (Douglas)"), a multi-use [stadium](/wiki/Stadium "Stadium") located on Pulrose Road is owned by Douglas Borough Council.

A free [Parkrun](/wiki/Parkrun "Parkrun") takes place in Nobles Park, St. Ninian's Road every Saturday.^[\[49\]](#cite_note-49)^{#cite_ref-49}
