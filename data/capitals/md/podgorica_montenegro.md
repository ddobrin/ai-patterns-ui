**Podgorica** ^[\[a\]](#cite_note-native_name_1-3)^^[\[b\]](#cite_note-native_name_2-4)^ ([Montenegrin Cyrillic](/wiki/Montenegrin_Cyrillic_alphabet "Montenegrin Cyrillic alphabet"): Подгорица,^[\[c\]](#cite_note-native_name_4-5)^
pronounced [\[pǒdɡoritsa\]](/wiki/Help:IPA/Serbo-Croatian "Help:IPA/Serbo-Croatian");  'under the hill') is the [capital](/wiki/Capital_city "Capital city") and [largest city](/wiki/List_of_cities_and_towns_in_Montenegro "List of cities and towns in Montenegro") of [Montenegro](/wiki/Montenegro "Montenegro"). The city is just north of [Lake Skadar](/wiki/Lake_Skadar "Lake Skadar") and close to coastal destinations on the [Adriatic Sea](/wiki/Adriatic_Sea "Adriatic Sea"). Historically, it was Podgorica's position at the confluence of the [Ribnica](/wiki/Ribnica_River "Ribnica River") and [Morača](/wiki/Mora%C4%8Da_River "Morača River") rivers and at the meeting-point of the fertile [Zeta Plain](/wiki/Zeta_Plain "Zeta Plain") and [Bjelopavlići Valley](/wiki/Bjelopavli%C4%87i_Valley "Bjelopavlići Valley") that encouraged settlement. The surrounding landscape is predominantly mountainous terrain.{#cite_ref-native_name_4_5-0}

After [World War II](/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), Podgorica was first designated as the capital of Montenegro in 1946. At that time, it was renamed Titograd in honor of [Josip Broz Tito](/wiki/Josip_Broz_Tito "Josip Broz Tito"), the leader of [Yugoslavia](/wiki/Yugoslavia "Yugoslavia"). It served as the capital of the [Socialist Republic of Montenegro](/wiki/Socialist_Republic_of_Montenegro "Socialist Republic of Montenegro") within the Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia until Montenegro's declaration of independence in 2006, after which it was reaffirmed as the capital of an independent Montenegro. The city's original name, Podgorica, was restored in 1992 following the dissolution of Yugoslavia.

The economy of Podgorica is based on a mix of industries, including manufacturing, trade, and services. The city has seen a rise in the IT sector, with numerous startups and tech companies emerging. Podgorica serves as a central hub for Montenegro's transportation network, with an extensive road and rail system and the nearby [Podgorica Airport](/wiki/Podgorica_Airport "Podgorica Airport"). Tourism is also a growing sector, with the city being a gateway to Montenegro's natural and cultural attractions.

Podgorica is home to the [University of Montenegro](/wiki/University_of_Montenegro "University of Montenegro"), the largest and most significant educational institution in the country. The city's cultural institutions include the Montenegrin National Theatre, the Natural History Museum of Montenegro, and several galleries and libraries. About one-third of the city's area is composed of parks, gardens, and natural landscapes, including the nearby Gorica Hill.

Prominent landmarks in Podgorica include the Millennium Bridge, the Clock Tower, and the Cathedral of the Resurrection of Christ. The city also features historical sites such as the ancient Roman settlement of Doclea and the old Ottoman town of Stara Varoš.  

Etymology {#Etymology}
----------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Etymology")\]

Podgorica is written in [Cyrillic](/wiki/Cyrillic "Cyrillic") as **Подгорица** ,
pronounced [\[pǒdɡoritsa\]](/wiki/Help:IPA/Serbo-Croatian "Help:IPA/Serbo-Croatian"); [UK](/wiki/British_English "British English"): [/ˈpɒdɡɒrɪtsə, pɒdˈɡɔːr-/](/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English"),^[\[3\]](#cite_note-6)^ [US](/wiki/American_English "American English"): [/ˈpɒdɡəriːtsə, ˈpɔːdɡɒr-/](/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English");^[\[4\]](#cite_note-7)^^[\[5\]](#cite_note-8)^^[\[6\]](#cite_note-9)^ *Podgorica* literally means "under the hill". *Gorica* ([Cyrillic](/wiki/Cyrillic_script "Cyrillic script"):
Горица), a diminutive of the word Gora (Cyrillic: Гора) which is another word for Mountain or Hill, means "little/small hill", is the name of one of the [cypress](/wiki/Cypress "Cypress")-covered [hillocks](/wiki/Hillock "Hillock") that overlooks the city center. Some three kilometres (1.9 miles) north-west of Podgorica lie the ruins of the Roman-era town of [Doclea](/wiki/Doclea_(city) "Doclea (city)"), from which the [Roman Emperor](/wiki/Roman_Emperor "Roman Emperor") [Diocletian](/wiki/Diocletian "Diocletian")'s mother hailed. In later centuries, Romans corrected the name to *Dioclea*, guessing that an *i* had been lost in vulgar speech. *[Duklja](/wiki/Duklja "Duklja")* is the later [South Slavic](/wiki/South_Slavic_languages "South Slavic languages") version of the same word. At its foundation in 426 AD,^[\[7\]](#cite_note-10)^ the town was called [**Birziminium**](/wiki/Birziminium "Birziminium") \[[sr](https://sr.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%91%D0%B8%D1%80%D0%B7%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%B8%D0%BD%D0%B8%D1%98%D1%83%D0%BC "sr:Бирзиминијум")\]. In the [Middle Ages](/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"), it was known as **Ribnica** ([Cyrillic](/wiki/Cyrillic_script "Cyrillic script"):
Рибница, [\[rîbnitsa\]](/wiki/Help:IPA/Serbo-Croatian "Help:IPA/Serbo-Croatian")). The name Podgorica was used from 1326. From 1946 to 1992, the city was [named](/wiki/List_of_places_named_after_Tito "List of places named after Tito") **Titograd** ([Cyrillic](/wiki/Cyrillic_script "Cyrillic script"):
Титоград, [\[tîtoɡraːd\]](/wiki/Help:IPA/Serbo-Croatian "Help:IPA/Serbo-Croatian")) in honour of [Josip Broz Tito](/wiki/Josip_Broz_Tito "Josip Broz Tito"), the [President](/wiki/List_of_heads_of_state_of_Yugoslavia#SFR_Yugoslavia "List of heads of state of Yugoslavia") of [Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia](/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia "Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia") from 1953 to 1980. In 1992 the city changed its name to "Podgorica", which it remains today.{#cite_ref-10}

### Early history {#Early_history}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Early history")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Doclea_Roman_city_Podgorica.jpg/220px-Doclea_Roman_city_Podgorica.jpg)](/wiki/File:Doclea_Roman_city_Podgorica.jpg) [Doclea](/wiki/Doclea_(Illyria) "Doclea (Illyria)"), Roman town, the seat of the Late Roman province of [Praevalitana](/wiki/Praevalitana "Praevalitana") [![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Ancient_city_Doclea_-_ruins_06.jpg/190px-Ancient_city_Doclea_-_ruins_06.jpg)](/wiki/File:Ancient_city_Doclea_-_ruins_06.jpg) Archeological remains of the Roman period in Doclea

Podgorica is at the [crossroads](/wiki/Crossroads_(culture) "Crossroads (culture)") of several historically important routes, near the rivers [Zeta](/wiki/Zeta_River "Zeta River"), [Morača](/wiki/Mora%C4%8Da "Morača"), [Cijevna](/wiki/Cijevna "Cijevna"), [Ribnica](/wiki/Ribnica_(river) "Ribnica (river)"), [Sitnica](/wiki/Sitnica_(Mora%C4%8Da) "Sitnica (Morača)") and [Mareza](/wiki/Mareza "Mareza") in the valley of [Lake Skadar](/wiki/Lake_Skadar "Lake Skadar") and near the Adriatic Sea, in fertile lowlands with favourable climate. The earliest human settlements were in prehistory: the oldest physical remains are from the late [Stone Age](/wiki/Stone_Age "Stone Age").

In the [Iron Age](/wiki/Iron_Age "Iron Age"), the area between the [Zeta](/wiki/Zeta_plain "Zeta plain") and [Bjelopavlići valleys](/wiki/Bjelopavli%C4%87i_plain "Bjelopavlići plain") was populated by two Illyrian tribes, the [Labeates](/wiki/Labeates "Labeates") and the [Docleatae](/wiki/Docleatae "Docleatae"). The population of the town of Doclea was 8,000--10,000, in which all core urban issues were resolved. The high population density (in an area of about 10 km (6 mi) radius) was made possible by the geographical position, favorable climate, and economic conditions and by the defensive positions that were of great importance at that time.

The name Podgorica was first mentioned in 1326 in a court document of the [Kotor](/wiki/Kotor "Kotor") archives. The city was economically strong: trade routes between the [Republic of Ragusa](/wiki/Republic_of_Ragusa "Republic of Ragusa") and Serbia, well developed at that time, were maintained via the road that led to Podgorica through [Trebinje](/wiki/Trebinje "Trebinje") and [Nikšić](/wiki/Nik%C5%A1i%C4%87 "Nikšić"). As a busy crossroads, Podgorica was a vibrant regional center of trade and communication. This boosted its development, economic power, military strength, and strategic importance.

In 1448 [Stefan Crnojević](/wiki/Stefan_Crnojevi%C4%87 "Stefan Crnojević") allied himself with the [Republic of Venice](/wiki/Republic_of_Venice "Republic of Venice"), and after defeating the despot [Đurađ Branković](/wiki/%C4%90ura%C4%91_Brankovi%C4%87 "Đurađ Branković") in 1452, he handed over Podgorica to the Venetian Republic, which already held the [Shkodër](/wiki/Shkod%C3%ABr "Shkodër") region. However, Venetian rule of Podgorica did not last long, because [Mehmed II](/wiki/Mehmed_II "Mehmed II") soon conquered the fortified town of [Medun](/wiki/Medun "Medun") in 1455, and two years later, most of [Zeta](/wiki/Zeta_under_the_Crnojevi%C4%87i "Zeta under the Crnojevići").^[\[8\]](#cite_note-11)^{#cite_ref-11}  

### Ottoman Empire {#Ottoman_Empire}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Ottoman Empire")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/28iulsah.jpg/220px-28iulsah.jpg)](/wiki/File:28iulsah.jpg) View of [Ribnica fortress and Old bridge](/wiki/Depedogen "Depedogen"), Catholic Church (right), Debbaglar Bridge, government mansion and the Mirko Varosh Hotel (far left), before 1901

The [Ottoman Empire](/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") captured Podgorica in 1474. Podgorica became a [kaza](/wiki/Qadaa "Qadaa") of the [Sanjak of Scutari](/wiki/Sanjak_of_Scutari "Sanjak of Scutari") (which was historically led by Albanian Pashas). In 1479, The Ottomans built a [large fortress](/wiki/Depedogen "Depedogen") in Podgorica, and the existing settlement, with its highly developed merchant connections, became the main Ottoman defensive and attacking bastion in the region. At the beginning of 1474 the Ottoman sultan intended to rebuild Podgorica and [Baleč](/wiki/Bale%C4%8D "Baleč") and settle them with 5,000 Muslim families (most of them of Albanian or Slavic origin),^[\[9\]](#cite_note-12)^ in order to stop cooperation between the [Principality of Zeta](/wiki/Principality_of_Zeta "Principality of Zeta") and [Albania Veneta](/wiki/Albania_Veneta "Albania Veneta").^[\[10\]](#cite_note-13)^{#cite_ref-13}

Podgorica fell again, but this time to the Ottomans in 1484, and the character of the town changed extensively. The Ottomans fortified the city, building towers, gates, and defensive ramparts that give Podgorica the appearance of an Ottoman military city.
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Natural_History_Museum_of_Montenegro%2C_Sahat_Kula%2C_2019.jpg/180px-Natural_History_Museum_of_Montenegro%2C_Sahat_Kula%2C_2019.jpg)](/wiki/File:Natural_History_Museum_of_Montenegro,_Sahat_Kula,_2019.jpg) [City Clock Tower](/wiki/Clock_Tower_(Podgorica) "Clock Tower (Podgorica)")

Most of today's Montenegro and Podgorica fell under the rule of the Albanian [Bushati](/wiki/Bushati "Bushati") Family of Shkodra between 1760 and 1831, which ruled independently from the Imperial authority of the Ottoman Sultan.

In 1864, Podgorica became a *kaza* of the [Scutari Vilayet](/wiki/Scutari_Vilayet "Scutari Vilayet") called Böğürtlen ("blackberry", also known as Burguriçe).
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Sastavci_Podgorica.jpg/220px-Sastavci_Podgorica.jpg)](/wiki/File:Sastavci_Podgorica.jpg) View of [Ribnica fortress and Old bridge](/wiki/Depedogen "Depedogen")

On 7 October 1874, in a violent reaction over the murder of a local named Juso Mučin Krnić,^[\[11\]](#cite_note-MJ-14)^ Ottoman forces killed at least 15 people in Podgorica.^[\[12\]](#cite_note-INPP-15)^ The massacre was widely reported outside of Montenegro and ultimately contributed to the buildup to the [Montenegrin-Ottoman War](/wiki/Montenegrin%E2%80%93Ottoman_War_(1876%E2%80%9378) "Montenegrin–Ottoman War (1876–78)").{#cite_ref-INPP_15-0}

The end of the Montenegrin-Ottoman War in 1878 resulted in the [Congress of Berlin](/wiki/Congress_of_Berlin "Congress of Berlin") recognizing vast territories, including that of Podgorica, as part of the newly recognized [Principality of Montenegro](/wiki/Principality_of_Montenegro "Principality of Montenegro"). At that time there were about 1,500 houses in Podgorica, with more than 8,000 people living there -- of Orthodox, Roman Catholic, and Muslim faiths flourishing together.  

### The Petrović and Karađorđević monarchies {#The_Petrovi.C4.87_and_Kara.C4.91or.C4.91evi.C4.87_monarchies}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: The Petrović and Karađorđević monarchies")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/King_Nicholas_I_Monument_2.jpg/190px-King_Nicholas_I_Monument_2.jpg)](/wiki/File:King_Nicholas_I_Monument_2.jpg) [King Nicholas I monument](/wiki/Nikola_I_Petrovi%C4%87-Njego%C5%A1 "Nikola I Petrović-Njegoš")

After the [Berlin Congress](/wiki/Berlin_Congress "Berlin Congress") in 1878, Podgorica was annexed to the Principality of Montenegro, marking the end of four centuries of Ottoman rule, and the beginning of a new era for Podgorica and Montenegro. The first forms of capital concentration were seen in 1902 when roads were built to all neighboring towns, and tobacco became Podgorica's first significant commercial product. In 1904, a [savings bank](/wiki/Savings_bank "Savings bank") named Zetska formed the first significant financial institution; this would soon grow into Podgorička Bank.

[World War I](/wiki/Balkans_Campaign_(World_War_I) "Balkans Campaign (World War I)") marked the end of dynamic development for Podgorica, which by then was the largest city in the newly proclaimed [Kingdom of Montenegro](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Montenegro "Kingdom of Montenegro"). On 10 August 1914, nine military personnel and 13 civilians were killed in Podgorica from an aerial bombardment by [Austro-Hungarian Aviation Troops](/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Aviation_Troops "Austro-Hungarian Aviation Troops").^[\[13\]](#cite_note-NBP-16)^ The city was bombed three more times in 1915.^[\[13\]](#cite_note-NBP-16)^ Along with the rest of the Kingdom, Podgorica was occupied by [Austria-Hungary](/wiki/Austria-Hungary "Austria-Hungary") from 1916 to 1918.{#cite_ref-NBP_16-1}

After the liberation by the [Allies](/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I "Allies of World War I") in 1918, the controversial [Podgorica Assembly](/wiki/Podgorica_Assembly "Podgorica Assembly") marked the end of Montenegrin statehood, as Montenegro was merged with the [Kingdom of Serbia](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Serbia "Kingdom of Serbia") and incorporated into the [Kingdom of Serbs, Croats, and Slovenes](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Yugoslavia "Kingdom of Yugoslavia"). The population of urban Podgorica during this interwar period was approximately 14,000.^[\[14\]](#cite_note-PK-17)^{#cite_ref-PK_17-0}

During the interwar period (1918--1941), Podgorica had public bathrooms as most residents did not have their own.^[\[14\]](#cite_note-PK-17)^ However, the *Imperial* hotel built in 1925 had two bathrooms, which was unprecedented at the time.^[\[14\]](#cite_note-PK-17)^ It was one of at least six hotels built in the city during the interwar period.^[\[14\]](#cite_note-PK-17)^{#cite_ref-PK_17-3}  

### World War II {#World_War_II}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: World War II")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Aerial_photo_of_bombing_of_Podgorica.JPG/220px-Aerial_photo_of_bombing_of_Podgorica.JPG)](/wiki/File:Aerial_photo_of_bombing_of_Podgorica.JPG) [Bombing of Podgorica in World War II](/wiki/Bombing_of_Podgorica_in_World_War_II "Bombing of Podgorica in World War II")

After the [Yugoslav coup d'état](/wiki/Yugoslav_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "Yugoslav coup d'état") on 27 March 1941, demonstrations supporting the coup took place in Podgorica.^[\[15\]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorrison200951-18)^ As a result of the coup, Yugoslavia turned against its previous alliance with the Axis powers and was subsequently invaded. Podgorica was [bombed over 80 times](/wiki/Bombing_of_Podgorica_in_World_War_II "Bombing of Podgorica in World War II") throughout the course of the war.^[\[16\]](#cite_note-VTG-19)^ The city was first bombed by the [Luftwaffe](/wiki/Luftwaffe "Luftwaffe") on 6 April 1941. On 5 May 1944, Podgorica was bombed by the [USAAF](/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Force "United States Army Air Force") in an attack against Axis forces, although the bombardment that day killed approximately 400 civilians.^[\[17\]](#cite_note-Kovacevic57-20)^ The city was liberated on 19 December 1944. According to the *Museum of Genocide Victims* , a total of 1,691 people were killed in Podgorica over the course of the war.^[\[18\]](#cite_note-MAKRO-21)^{#cite_ref-MAKRO_21-0}  

### Socialist Yugoslavia {#Socialist_Yugoslavia}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Socialist Yugoslavia")\]

On 12 July 1946, [Josip Broz Tito](/wiki/Josip_Broz_Tito "Josip Broz Tito") made one of his early visits to Podgorica from the *Radovče* hotel, where he spoke to a crowd.^[\[19\]](#cite_note-DTV-22)^ It was the first of fifteen total visits made by Tito to the city after World War II.^[\[19\]](#cite_note-DTV-22)^{#cite_ref-DTV_22-1}
> "Podgorica is destroyed. We will build her altogether because it's our responsibility, because that's what's required of us by the sacrifices which Podgorica gave! We will do it, that's what I promise you in the name of the Federal government." -[Josip Broz Tito](/wiki/Josip_Broz_Tito "Josip Broz Tito") on 12 July 1946.^[\[19\]](#cite_note-DTV-22)^{#cite_ref-DTV_22-2}

On 25 July 1948, the vice president of the People's Parliament of Montenegro, Andrija Mugoša, along with secretary Gavron Cemović, signed a law changing the name of Podgorica into "Titovgrad".^[\[16\]](#cite_note-VTG-19)^ The law was "retroactively" activated such that the name change applied to any records starting from 13 July 1946, when it became the capital of [Montenegro](/wiki/Socialist_Republic_of_Montenegro "Socialist Republic of Montenegro") within [the newly formed Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia](/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia "Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia").^[\[16\]](#cite_note-VTG-19)^ However, in a contradiction, the "Službeni list" or legal code of Yugoslavia recorded the name "Titograd" without the letter "v".^[\[16\]](#cite_note-VTG-19)^ Ultimately, "Titograd" was used over "Titovgrad".^[\[16\]](#cite_note-VTG-19)^{#cite_ref-VTG_19-4}

In addition to the new name, Titograd saw the establishment of new factories. The Radoje Dakić factory, built-in 1946 for the production of heavy machinery, became one of the largest employers in Titograd. In 1964, Radoje Dakić guaranteed hired workers an apartment in the city.^[\[20\]](#cite_note-Senat-23)^ In the late 1960s, the cities of Titograd, [Zadar](/wiki/Zadar "Zadar"), and [Mostar](/wiki/Mostar "Mostar") competed to be selected as the location of Yugoslavia's expanding aluminum industry.^[\[21\]](#cite_note-MKAP-24)^ In a highly politicized selection process, Titograd was ultimately chosen and the [Kombinat](/wiki/Aluminium_Plant_Podgorica "Aluminium Plant Podgorica") was constructed in 1969.^[\[21\]](#cite_note-MKAP-24)^ In 1974, the public [Veljko Vlahović University](/wiki/University_of_Montenegro "University of Montenegro") was founded in Titograd. On 15 April 1979, the city suffered damage by a [6.9 magnitude earthquake](/wiki/1979_Montenegro_earthquake "1979 Montenegro earthquake").{#cite_ref-MKAP_24-1}

Titograd was the site of massive protests during [Yugoslavia's anti-bureaucratic revolution](/wiki/Anti-bureaucratic_revolution "Anti-bureaucratic revolution"). On 10 January 1989, over 10,000 people protested in the city.^[\[22\]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMilosavljević2003-25)^^[\[23\]](#cite_note-Vreme-26)^ By the turn of the decade, Titograd was recognized as the city with the most greenery in Yugoslavia, along with [Banja Luka](/wiki/Banja_Luka "Banja Luka").^[\[24\]](#cite_note-MZ-27)^{#cite_ref-MZ_27-0}  

### Contemporary history {#Contemporary_history}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Contemporary history")\]

As [Yugoslavia began to break up](/wiki/Breakup_of_Yugoslavia "Breakup of Yugoslavia"), Titograd was renamed to Podgorica after a referendum on 2 April 1992.^[\[25\]](#cite_note-ZU-28)^ On 25 May 1992, Podgorica was the site of a [Serbian Radical Party](/wiki/Serbian_Radical_Party "Serbian Radical Party") rally of approximately 10,000 supporters, during which a [Montenegrin Bosniak](/wiki/Bosniaks_of_Montenegro "Bosniaks of Montenegro") man named Adem Šabotić attempted to assassinate [Vojislav Šešelj](/wiki/Vojislav_%C5%A0e%C5%A1elj "Vojislav Šešelj") via hand bomb after his supporters chanted references to killing [Muslims](/wiki/Muslims "Muslims").^[\[26\]](#cite_note-BHASPG-29)^ Šešelj, his bodyguards, and a few bystanders were injured after the bomb detonated but no one was killed.^[\[26\]](#cite_note-BHASPG-29)^{#cite_ref-BHASPG_29-1}

Otherwise, the [Yugoslav wars](/wiki/Yugoslav_wars "Yugoslav wars") largely bypassed Podgorica, but the entire country was greatly affected with severe economic stagnation and hyperinflation lasting throughout the 1990s due to [international sanctions](/wiki/Sanctions_against_Yugoslavia "Sanctions against Yugoslavia"). In 1999, Podgorica was subject to airstrikes during the [NATO bombing of Yugoslavia](/wiki/1999_NATO_bombing_of_Yugoslavia "1999 NATO bombing of Yugoslavia").^[\[27\]](#cite_note-APPN-30)^^[\[28\]](#cite_note-WPPN-31)^{#cite_ref-WPPN_31-0}

On 13 July 2005, the newly constructed [Millennium Bridge](/wiki/Millennium_Bridge_(Montenegro) "Millennium Bridge (Montenegro)") opened for traffic.^[\[29\]](#cite_note-ekm-32)^ Following the results of the [independence referendum](/wiki/2006_Montenegrin_independence_referendum "2006 Montenegrin independence referendum") in May 2006, Podgorica saw significant development as the capital of an independent state, including the reconstruction and renaming of the former Ivan Milutinović Square to [Independence Square](/wiki/Independence_Square_(Podgorica) "Independence Square (Podgorica)").{#cite_ref-ekm_32-0}

On 13 October 2008, at least 10,000 people [protested](/wiki/2008_Podgorica_protest "2008 Podgorica protest") against [Kosovo's declaration of independence](/wiki/2008_Kosovo_declaration_of_independence "2008 Kosovo declaration of independence").^[\[30\]](#cite_note-DWKP-33)^ On 19 December 2008, the [Moscow Bridge](/wiki/Moscow_Bridge "Moscow Bridge") opened for pedestrians.{#cite_ref-DWKP_33-0}

On 7 August 2013, the 60-year old Hotel *Crna Gora* was demolished to make way for the new [Hilton](/wiki/Hilton_Hotels_%26_Resorts "Hilton Hotels & Resorts") in its place, which opened in 2016.^[\[31\]](#cite_note-KHCGPG-34)^ Construction of the [Cathedral of Christ's Resurrection](/wiki/Cathedral_of_the_Resurrection_of_Christ,_Podgorica "Cathedral of the Resurrection of Christ, Podgorica") finished after 20 years on 7 October 2013.{#cite_ref-KHCGPG_34-0}

In October 2015, [protests](/wiki/2015%E2%80%932016_Montenegrin_crisis "2015–2016 Montenegrin crisis") took place in Podgorica ahead of [Montenegro's accession into NATO](/wiki/Montenegro%E2%80%93NATO_relations "Montenegro–NATO relations"). After a demonstration of at least 5,000 to 8,000 people,^[\[32\]](#cite_note-DWKC-35)^ the police used [tear gas](/wiki/Tear_gas "Tear gas") to disperse demonstrators from the [parliament](/wiki/Parliament_of_Montenegro "Parliament of Montenegro").^[\[33\]](#cite_note-VPDF-36)^ Protests in the city continued through the [2016 Montenegrin parliamentary election](/wiki/2016_Montenegrin_parliamentary_election "2016 Montenegrin parliamentary election"). On 22 February 2018, a [Yugoslav Army](/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_Serbia_and_Montenegro "Armed Forces of Serbia and Montenegro") veteran [killed himself at the US embassy in Podgorica](/wiki/2018_United_States_embassy_attack_in_Podgorica "2018 United States embassy attack in Podgorica").{#cite_ref-VPDF_36-0}  

Administration {#Administration}
--------------------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Administration")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/PodgoricaUrbanSubdivisions.png/220px-PodgoricaUrbanSubdivisions.png)](/wiki/File:PodgoricaUrbanSubdivisions.png) [Podgorica urban subdivisions](/wiki/List_of_Podgorica_neighbourhoods_and_suburbs "List of Podgorica neighbourhoods and suburbs") [![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Podgorica_Municipal_building.jpg/220px-Podgorica_Municipal_building.jpg)](/wiki/File:Podgorica_Municipal_building.jpg) Podgorica City Hall and the monument to [Marko Miljanov Popović](/wiki/Marko_Miljanov_Popovi%C4%87 "Marko Miljanov Popović")

The city administration consists of a mayor, city [assembly](/wiki/Deliberative_assembly "Deliberative assembly"), and a number of secretariats and administrative bodies which together act as a city local government. The city assembly has 61 members, elected directly for four-year terms. The mayor used to be directly elected for a five-year term, but since the new law was introduced in Montenegrin municipalities mayors will be elected by the city assembly and will have to maintain its support during the term. Separate elections are held for the local sub-division of [Golubovci](/wiki/Golubovci "Golubovci") since it is part of their administrative autonomy inside Podgorica municipality. Constant questions are raised by various politicians over gaining separate municipality status for [Golubovci](/wiki/Golubovci "Golubovci"). In 2018, [Tuzi](/wiki/Tuzi_Municipality "Tuzi Municipality") became its own municipality after a vote on the Montenegrin Parliament.^[\[34\]](#cite_note-37)^{#cite_ref-37}

On local elections held on 25 May 2014, the [Democratic Party of Socialists](/wiki/Democratic_Party_of_Socialists "Democratic Party of Socialists") won 29 seats in the municipal assembly, one short of 30 needed to form a majority. [Democratic Front](/wiki/Democratic_Front_(Montenegro) "Democratic Front (Montenegro)") won 17 seats, [SNP](/wiki/Socialist_People%27s_Party_of_Montenegro "Socialist People's Party of Montenegro") won 8 seats, while coalition made of [Positive Montenegro](/wiki/Positive_Montenegro "Positive Montenegro") and [SDP](/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Montenegro "Social Democratic Party of Montenegro") won 5 seats. After lengthy negotiations, SDP dissolved coalition with Pozitivna and made an arrangement on forming a majority with DPS, similar to one they have in national government. While SDP is a longtime partner of DPS at the national level, it has been in opposition to Podgorica municipal assembly in 2010--2014 period. Since October 2014, the position of the mayor is held by [DPS](/wiki/Democratic_Party_of_Socialists "Democratic Party of Socialists") official, Slavoljub Stijepović, replacing Podgorica mayor od 14 years, [Miomir Mugoša](/wiki/Miomir_Mugo%C5%A1a "Miomir Mugoša"). Since October 2018, the position of the Mayor is held by [DPS](/wiki/Democratic_Party_of_Socialists_of_Montenegro "Democratic Party of Socialists of Montenegro") Vice president [dr Ivan Vuković](/wiki/Ivan_Vukovi%C4%87_(politician) "Ivan Vuković (politician)"), replacing Slavoljub Stijepović. On 13 April 2023, [Olivera Injac](/wiki/Olivera_Injac "Olivera Injac") from [PES](/wiki/Europe_Now! "Europe Now!") was sworn in as mayor, thus becoming the first non-DPS mayor since 1998.

### Local subdivisions {#Local_subdivisions}

The entire municipality of Podgorica is further divided into 66 *[local communities](/wiki/Local_community "Local community")* (мјесне заједнице, *mjesne zajednice*), bodies in which the citizens participate in decisions on matters of relevance to the local community.  

Geography {#Geography}
----------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Geography")\]

Podgorica is located in central Montenegro. The area is crossed with rivers and the city itself is only 15 kilometres (9.3 mi) north of [Lake Skadar](/wiki/Lake_Skadar "Lake Skadar"). The [Morača](/wiki/Mora%C4%8Da_River "Morača River") and [Ribnica](/wiki/Ribnica_River "Ribnica River") rivers flow through the city, while the [Zeta](/wiki/Zeta_River "Zeta River"), [Cijevna](/wiki/Cijevna_River "Cijevna River"), [Sitnica](/wiki/Sitnica_River "Sitnica River") and [Mareza](/wiki/Mareza "Mareza") flow nearby. *Morača* is the largest river in the city, being 70 m or 230 ft wide near downtown, and having carved a 20 m or 66 ft deep canyon for the length of its course through the city. ^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^ Except for the Morača and Zeta, other rivers have an appearance of small creeks. The richness in bodies of water is a major feature of the city.

In contrast to most of Montenegro, Podgorica lies in a mainly flat area at the northern end of the Zeta plain, at an elevation of 40 m (130 ft). The only exceptions are hills which overlook the city. The most significant is 130.3 m (427 ft) high **[Gorica Hill](https://podgorica.travel/en/nature-parks-and-picnic-sports/)** (
pronounced [\[ˈɡǒrit͜sa\]](/wiki/Help:IPA/Serbo-Croatian "Help:IPA/Serbo-Croatian")), city's namesake, which rises above the city centre. The other hills include *Malo brdo* ("little hill", 205.4 m or 674 ft), *Velje brdo* ("big hill", 283 m or 928 ft), [**Ljubović**](https://podgorica.travel/en/nature-parks-and-picnic-sports/) (101 m or 331 ft) and *Dajbapska gora* (172 m or 564 ft). Podgorica city proper has an area of 108 square kilometres (42 sq mi), while actual urbanized area is much smaller.

### Climate {#Climate}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Climate")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Podgorica%2C_piazza_repubblica.JPG/180px-Podgorica%2C_piazza_repubblica.JPG)](/wiki/File:Podgorica,_piazza_repubblica.JPG) [Independence Square](/wiki/Republic_Square_(Podgorica) "Republic Square (Podgorica)").

Under the [Köppen climate classification](/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification "Köppen climate classification"), Podgorica is transitional between a [humid subtropical climate](/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate "Humid subtropical climate") (*Cfa* ) and a [hot-summer Mediterranean climate](/wiki/Hot-summer_Mediterranean_climate "Hot-summer Mediterranean climate") (*Csa* ), since the driest summer month gets slightly less than 40 millimeters (1.6 in) of precipitation,^[\[35\]](#cite_note-Peel-38)^ with summer highs around 34 °C (93 °F) and winter highs around 11 °C (52 °F). Although the city is only some 35 km (22 mi) north of the [Adriatic Sea](/wiki/Adriatic_Sea "Adriatic Sea"), an arm of the Mediterranean, [Mount Rumija](/wiki/Rumija "Rumija") acts as a natural barrier, separating Skadar Lake basin and Podgorica area from the sea, thus limiting temperate maritime influence on the local climate.{#cite_ref-Peel_38-0}

The mean annual rainfall is 1,659 mm (65.3 in), making Podgorica by far the wettest capital in Europe, [Ljubljana](/wiki/Ljubljana "Ljubljana") being second with 1,362 mm (53.6 in). The temperature exceeds 25 °C (77 °F) on about 135 days each year.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^ The number of rainy days is about 120, and those with a strong wind around 60.^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^ An occasional strong northerly wind influences the climate in the winter, with a [wind chill](/wiki/Wind_chill "Wind chill") effect lowering the perceived temperature by a few degrees. ^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^

The all-time maximum snowfall record was beaten on 11 February 2012, when 58 cm (23 in) of snowfall were measured. Before that, the biggest snowfall in Podgorica was in 1954, when 52 cm (20 in) of snowfall was recorded. Maximum temperature was recorded on 24 August 2007, at 44.8 °C (112.6 °F), while all time minimum was −9.7 °C (14.5 °F), on 4 February 1956.

With a population of 179,505,^[\[39\]](#cite_note-Monstat-42)^ Podgorica is by far the most populous city in Montenegro. Area of former Podgorica Municipality ([Podgorica Capital City](/wiki/Podgorica_Capital_City "Podgorica Capital City"), [Tuzi](/wiki/Tuzi_Municipality "Tuzi Municipality") and [Zeta](/wiki/Zeta_Municipality "Zeta Municipality") Municipalities) has a population of 208,555, while Podgorica urban area population is 173,024.{#cite_ref-Monstat_42-0}

According to 2023 census, Podgorica has 64,140 households and 88,431 dwellings. At the end of 2023, there were 90,799 vehicles registered in Podgorica, including 78,047 passenger vehicles and 1,564 motorcycles.

Out of the total population of Podgorica 47.81% are male and 52.19% are female. The average age of the population is 37.65. There are 11,626 foreign nationals in Podgorica, making up 6,5% of city population.

In October 2024, 112,482 residents of Podgorica were officially employed, [earning](/wiki/Net_income "Net income") 981€ on average.

Almost 92% of residents are speaking one of the mutually intelligible offshoots of [Serbo-Croatian language](/wiki/Serbo-Croatian_language "Serbo-Croatian language"), including [Montenegrin](/wiki/Montenegrin_language "Montenegrin language") and [Serbian language](/wiki/Serbian_language "Serbian language") .

Economy {#Economy}
------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Economy")\]

Podgorica is not only the administrative center of Montenegro but also its main economic engine. Most of Montenegro's industrial, financial, and commercial base is in Podgorica. ^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^

Before World War I, most of Podgorica's economy was in trade and small-scale manufacturing, which was an economic model established during the long rule of the [Ottoman Empire](/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire"). ^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^ After World War II, Podgorica became Montenegro's capital and a focus of the rapid urbanization and industrialization of the SFRY era. Industries such as aluminium and tobacco processing, [textiles](/wiki/Textile_industry "Textile industry"), engineering, vehicle production, and wine production were established in and around the city. In 1981, Podgorica's GDP per capita was 87% of the Yugoslav average.^[\[41\]](#cite_note-44)^{#cite_ref-44}
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Central_Bank_of_Montenegro.jpg/220px-Central_Bank_of_Montenegro.jpg)](/wiki/File:Central_Bank_of_Montenegro.jpg) Headquarters of the [Central Bank of Montenegro](/wiki/Central_Bank_of_Montenegro "Central Bank of Montenegro")

In the early 1990s, the [dissolution of Yugoslavia](/wiki/Breakup_of_Yugoslavia "Breakup of Yugoslavia"), [Yugoslav wars](/wiki/Yugoslav_wars "Yugoslav wars"), and the [UN-imposed sanctions](/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_757 "United Nations Security Council Resolution 757") left Podgorica's industries without traditional markets, suppliers, and available funds. This, combined with typical [transition](/wiki/Transition_economy "Transition economy") pressures, led to a decline of the industrial base, where many industries collapsed leaving thousands of citizens unemployed. ^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^ However, some of the industries, including [Plantaže](/wiki/Planta%C5%BEe "Plantaže"), managed to survive the turmoil of the 1990s, and are still major contributors to Montenegrin export and industrial output to this day.

As Montenegro began its push for independence from Serbia in the late 1990s, Podgorica greatly benefited from the increased concentration of government and service sectors. ^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^ In addition to almost the entire country's government, Podgorica is home to the [Montenegro Stock Exchange](/wiki/Montenegro_Stock_Exchange "Montenegro Stock Exchange") and other major Montenegrin financial institutions, along with telecommunications carriers, media outlets, [Montenegrin flag carrier airline](/wiki/Air_Montenegro "Air Montenegro"), and other significant institutions and companies.

The large presence of government and service sectors spared the economy of Podgorica from prolonged stagnation in the [late 2000s recession](/wiki/Great_Recession "Great Recession"), which hit Montenegro hard. Although in mid-2014, some 30% of Montenegro's citizens lived in Podgorica, the municipality accounted for 44% of the country's employed. Out of the entire mass of paid net salaries in Montenegro in that year, some 47% was paid in Podgorica. The average monthly net salary in December 2021 was €537 in Podgorica municipality.^[\[42\]](#cite_note-45)^{#cite_ref-45}

### Media {#Media}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Media")\]

Podgorica is the media hub of Montenegro. It is home to the headquarters of the state-owned [public television](/wiki/Public_television "Public television") broadcaster [RTCG](/wiki/Radio_and_Television_of_Montenegro "Radio and Television of Montenegro"). Transport {#Transport}
----------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Transport")\]  

### Public transport {#Public_transport}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Public transport")\]

Public transport in Podgorica consists of 11 urban and 16 suburban bus lines.^[\[44\]](#cite_note-47)^ The city-owned *AD Gradski saobraćaj* public transport company used to be the sole bus operator until the 1990s, when private carriers were introduced. The company went bankrupt in 2001, and buses were since operated solely by private carriers. As of 2023 Public transport in Podgorica is done only by buses, 12 city and 16 suburban lines.{#cite_ref-47}

Public transport faces competition from very popular dispatched taxi services. De-regulation. Usually, taxi companies provide a high level of service, with relatively new and uniform car fleets and [GPS](/wiki/GPS "GPS")-tracked vehicles.  

### Roads {#Roads}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Roads")\]

Podgorica's location in central Montenegro makes it a natural hub for rail and road transport. Roads in Montenegro (especially those connecting Podgorica to northern Montenegro and [Serbia](/wiki/Serbia "Serbia")) are usually inferior to modern European roads. Both major Montenegrin motorway projects, [Bar-Boljare motorway](/wiki/A-1_motorway_(Montenegro) "A-1 motorway (Montenegro)") and [Nudo--Božaj motorway](/wiki/Adriatic%E2%80%93Ionian_motorway#Montenegro "Adriatic–Ionian motorway"), will pass near Podgorica. The first phase of motorway A-1 (Bar-Boljare) was opened on July 13, 2022. The [Sozina tunnel](/wiki/Sozina_tunnel "Sozina tunnel") (4.2 km) shortened the journey from Podgorica to [Bar](/wiki/Bar,_Montenegro "Bar, Montenegro") (Montenegro's main seaport) to under 30 minutes. A new road bypass had been constructed in 2011, to remove transport routes from north to south of the country, out of the city center. A south-western bypass had also been constructed with the same goal of moving heavy transport out of the city core. Podgorica is also characteristic of its extensive network of multi-lane boulevards which make inner-city transport quick and effective. Traffic over the [Morača River](/wiki/Mora%C4%8Da_River "Morača River") also goes fluently since river banks are very well connected with 6 vehicular and 3 pedestrian bridges.

The main transit connections of Podgorica are:

* north ([E65](/wiki/European_route_E65 "European route E65"), [E80](/wiki/European_route_E80 "European route E80")), towards [Belgrade](/wiki/Belgrade "Belgrade") and on to Central Europe
* west ([E762](/wiki/European_route_E762 "European route E762")), towards [Nikšić](/wiki/Nik%C5%A1i%C4%87 "Nikšić"), [Bosnia](/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina "Bosnia and Herzegovina") and on to Western Europe
* south ([E65](/wiki/European_route_E65 "European route E65"), [E80](/wiki/European_route_E80 "European route E80")) towards the [Adriatic coast](/wiki/Adriatic_coast "Adriatic coast")
* east ([E762](/wiki/European_route_E762 "European route E762")), towards [Albania](/wiki/Albania "Albania")

### Rail {#Rail}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Rail")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/01.10.13_Podgorica_6111.101_%26_461.040_%2810101220063%29.jpg/220px-01.10.13_Podgorica_6111.101_%26_461.040_%2810101220063%29.jpg)](/wiki/File:01.10.13_Podgorica_6111.101_%26_461.040_(10101220063).jpg) [Podgorica Rail Station](/wiki/Podgorica_Rail_Station "Podgorica Rail Station")

Podgorica is a hub of the X-shaped [Montenegrin rail network](/wiki/Railways_of_Montenegro "Railways of Montenegro"). The [Belgrade--Bar](/wiki/Belgrade%E2%80%93Bar_railway "Belgrade–Bar railway") line converges with the [line to Nikšić](/wiki/Nik%C5%A1i%C4%87-Podgorica_railway "Nikšić-Podgorica railway") and [line to Shkodër](/wiki/Podgorica%E2%80%93Shkod%C3%ABr_railway "Podgorica–Shkodër railway") at the [Podgorica Rail Station](/wiki/Podgorica_Rail_Station "Podgorica Rail Station"). The station itself is located 1.5 km (0.93 mi) to the southeast of the main city square. Podgorica's main railway link (for both passenger and freight traffic) is Belgrade--Bar. The link to [Nikšić](/wiki/Nik%C5%A1i%C4%87 "Nikšić") was recently under reconstruction ([electrification](/wiki/Railway_electrification "Railway electrification"));^[\[45\]](#cite_note-48)^ afterwards, passenger service started in October 2012. The rail link to [Shkodër](/wiki/Shkod%C3%ABr "Shkodër") is used as freight-only.{#cite_ref-48}  

### Air {#Air}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Air")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Podgorica_airport.jpg/220px-Podgorica_airport.jpg)](/wiki/File:Podgorica_airport.jpg) [Podgorica Airport](/wiki/Podgorica_Airport "Podgorica Airport") near [Golubovci](/wiki/Golubovci "Golubovci").

[Podgorica Airport](/wiki/Podgorica_Airport "Podgorica Airport") is located in [Zeta Plain](/wiki/Zeta_Plain "Zeta Plain"), 11 km (6.8 mi) south of Podgorica City centre, and is Montenegro's main international airport. The airport is locally known as Golubovci Airport (Аеродром Голубовци / Aerodrom Golubovci), as it is located within the administrative boundaries of the town of [Golubovci](/wiki/Golubovci "Golubovci"). The IATA code of the airport is still TGD because Podgorica was named Titograd, during which time the airport opened. It is the main hub for [Air Montenegro](/wiki/Air_Montenegro "Air Montenegro") and [Di Air](/wiki/Di_Air "Di Air").

Culture {#Culture}
------------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Culture")\]
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/CNPPG.jpg/220px-CNPPG.jpg)](/wiki/File:CNPPG.jpg) [Montenegrin National Theatre](/wiki/Montenegrin_National_Theatre "Montenegrin National Theatre").

Podgorica is home to many Montenegrin cultural institutions and events. It hosts the [Montenegrin National Theatre](/wiki/Montenegrin_National_Theatre "Montenegrin National Theatre") and a number of museums and galleries. The Montenegrin National Theatre is the most significant theatre not only in Podgorica but in all of Montenegro. Podgorica is also host to the City Theatre (*Gradsko pozorište* ), which includes the Children's Theatre and the Puppet Theatre. Although not as rich in museums and galleries as the historic royal capital [Cetinje](/wiki/Cetinje "Cetinje"), there are several noteworthy museums:

* The Podgorica City Museum (*Muzej grada Podgorice* ) preserves Podgorica's rich heritage. Founded in 1950, it has four categories: archaeological, ethnographic, historical, and cultural-historical. It houses artifacts that date back to the [Roman](/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire") and [Illyrian](/wiki/Illyria "Illyria") eras. ^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^
* The Archaeological Research Centre (*Centar za arheološka istraživanja*) was founded in 1961. Its mission is to gather, classify, restore and display archaeological sites.
* The [Marko Miljanov](/wiki/Marko_Miljanov "Marko Miljanov") Museum (*Muzej Marka Miljanova* ) in [Medun](/wiki/Medun "Medun") shows life in 19th century Montenegro.
* The Natural History Museum (*Prirodnjački muzej* ) displays specimens of Montenegrin flora and fauna. This museum has no exhibition space of its own, despite many proposals and initiatives to build one. ^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Podgorica%2C_museo_nazionale%2C_esterno_01.JPG/220px-Podgorica%2C_museo_nazionale%2C_esterno_01.JPG)](/wiki/File:Podgorica,_museo_nazionale,_esterno_01.JPG) [Podgorica City Museum](/wiki/National_Museum_of_Montenegro "National Museum of Montenegro").

There is a notable art gallery in the *Dvorac* [*Petrovića*](/wiki/House_of_Petrovi%C4%87 "House of Petrović") ([Petrović Castle](/wiki/Podgorica_Royal_Palace "Podgorica Royal Palace")) complex in Podgorica's largest public park. The palace hosted the [Gallery of the Non-Aligned Countries "Josip Broz Tito"](/wiki/Gallery_of_the_Non-Aligned_Countries_%22Josip_Broz_Tito%22 "Gallery of the Non-Aligned Countries \"Josip Broz Tito\"") between 1984 and 1995. [King Nicholas](/wiki/Nicholas_I_of_Montenegro "Nicholas I of Montenegro")'s castle, *Perjanički Dom* (House of the Honour Guard), castle chapel and surrounding buildings were converted to an art gallery in 1984. Since 1995, it has been part of the Modern Arts Centre (*Centar savremenih umjetnosti* ) and houses approximately 1,500 works of art. The historic Cinema of Culture (*Kino Kultura* ), which was founded in 1949, was closed in November 2008 due to continuous financial losses it generated. It was the only cinema in the city for 6 decades. The building of the former cinema will be converted to host the Podgorica City Theatre. Shortly after its closure, a [Ster-Kinekor](/wiki/Ster-Kinekor "Ster-Kinekor") (later acquired by [Cineplexx](/wiki/Cineplexx_Cinemas "Cineplexx Cinemas")) 6-screen [multiplex](/wiki/Multiplex_(movie_theater) "Multiplex (movie theater)") cinema opened at BIG Podgorica shopping mall.

A significant cultural institution of over fifty years' standing is the Budo Tomović Cultural-Informational Centre (*KIC Budo Tomović* ). It is a public institution that organizes various artistic events, including Podgorica Cultural Summer (*Podgoričko Kulturno Ljeto* ), FIAT -- International Alternative Theatre Festival (*Festival Internacionalnog Alternativnog Teatra* ), DEUS -- December Arts Scene (*Decembarska Umjetnička Scena*).  

Sport {#Sport}
--------------

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: Sport")\]

The most popular sports by far are [football](/wiki/Association_football "Association football") and [basketball](/wiki/Basketball "Basketball"). Basketball became especially popular with the success in the late 20th and early 21st centuries of [KK Budućnost](/wiki/KK_Budu%C4%87nost_Podgorica "KK Budućnost Podgorica"), both in [Regional](/wiki/ABA_League "ABA League") and European competitions.

Football in Podgorica has a long tradition associated with [Budućnost](/wiki/FK_Budu%C4%87nost_Podgorica "FK Budućnost Podgorica"). World-famous players [Predrag Mijatović](/wiki/Predrag_Mijatovi%C4%87 "Predrag Mijatović") and [Dejan Savićević](/wiki/Dejan_Savi%C4%87evi%C4%87 "Dejan Savićević") were born in Podgorica and made their debut in that team. The club [FK Zeta](/wiki/FK_Zeta "FK Zeta") from the Podgorica suburb of [Golubovci](/wiki/Golubovci "Golubovci") has also reached the former first league of Serbia and Montenegro. These clubs, along with [Sutjeska](/wiki/FK_Sutjeska_Nik%C5%A1i%C4%87 "FK Sutjeska Nikšić") from Nikšić, usually compete with each other for leading position in the [First League of Montenegro](/wiki/First_League_of_Montenegro "First League of Montenegro"). Other clubs from Podgorica and its surroundings play in the Montenegrin First League e.g. [OFK Titograd](/wiki/OFK_Titograd "OFK Titograd") \& [Kom](/wiki/FK_Kom "FK Kom"). One of the most popular clubs from the suburbs is [FK Ribnica](/wiki/FK_Ribnica "FK Ribnica") from [Konik](/wiki/Konik,_Montenegro "Konik, Montenegro"), [FK Zabjelo](/wiki/FK_Zabjelo "FK Zabjelo") from [Zabjelo](/wiki/Zabjelo "Zabjelo") and [FK Podgorica](/wiki/FK_Podgorica "FK Podgorica") from *Donja Gorica*.

The volleyball team [OK Budućnost](/wiki/OK_Budu%C4%87nost_Podgorica "OK Budućnost Podgorica") and the women's handball team [ŽRK Budućnost T-Mobile](/wiki/%C5%BDRK_Budu%C4%87nost_T-Mobile "ŽRK Budućnost T-Mobile") have had significant success in European competition. [Budućnost Podgorica](/wiki/Budu%C4%87nost_Podgorica "Budućnost Podgorica") is the most important sports club in Podgorica. Its name means *Future*.

[Chess](/wiki/Chess "Chess") is another popular sport and some famous global chess players, like [Slavko Dedić](/wiki/Slavko_Dedi%C4%87 "Slavko Dedić"), are born in Podgorica.

Sporting events like the annual [Podgorica Marathon, Coinis no limits Triathlon](/wiki/Podgorica_Marathon "Podgorica Marathon"), and the *Morača River* jumps attract international competitors. Podgorica was the host of [2009 FINA Men's Water Polo World League](/wiki/2009_FINA_Men%27s_Water_Polo_World_League "2009 FINA Men's Water Polo World League").  

### Venues {#Venues}

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Podgorica&action=edit&section=28 "Edit section: Venues")\]

Podgorica has a number of sporting venues; some are under reconstruction and expansion. The main ones are:

* [Podgorica City Stadium](/wiki/Podgorica_City_Stadium "Podgorica City Stadium"). It has a capacity of 11,264 and it is the home of [FK Budućnost Podgorica](/wiki/FK_Budu%C4%87nost_Podgorica "FK Budućnost Podgorica") and the [Montenegro national football team](/wiki/Montenegro_national_football_team "Montenegro national football team"). It is the only venue in Montenegro that complies with [FIFA](/wiki/FIFA "FIFA") standards for international football matches.
* [Morača Sports Center](/wiki/Mora%C4%8Da_Sports_Center "Morača Sports Center"), a multi-functional indoor sports facility. It has a capacity of 6,000 seats. It hosted one group of [EuroBasket 2005](/wiki/EuroBasket_2005 "EuroBasket 2005"), while other games were played in [Belgrade](/wiki/Belgrade_Arena "Belgrade Arena"), [Vršac](/wiki/Millennium_Centar "Millennium Centar"), and [Novi Sad](/wiki/Spens_Sports_Center "Spens Sports Center").
* Bemax Arena, indoor and outdoor sports facility. It has a capacity of 2,400 seats.

Almost every football club in Podgorica has its own stadium, although these are often only fields with small stands or no stands at all. Other notable venues are the [Stadion malih sportova](/wiki/Stadion_malih_sportova "Stadion malih sportova") under Gorica hill and the [sport shooting](/wiki/Sport_shooting "Sport shooting") range under Ljubović hill. There are many other sports facilities around the city, most notably [indoor soccer](/wiki/Indoor_soccer "Indoor soccer") fields.

Podgorica's mixture of architectural styles reflects the turbulent history of the city and country: as one régime replaced another, the corresponding style was introduced.

As part of the [Ottoman Empire](/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") until 1878, Podgorica has some examples of Ottoman architecture. ^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^ The oldest parts of the city, [*Stara Varoš* (*Old town*)](https://podgorica.travel/en/monuments-of-culture/) and *Drač* is typical of this, with two mosques, a Turkish [**Clock Tower**](/wiki/Clock_Tower_(Podgorica) "Clock Tower (Podgorica)") and narrow, winding streets.

When the city was incorporated to Montenegro, the urban core shifted to the other bank of the [Ribnica River](/wiki/Ribnica_River "Ribnica River"), where the town developed in a more European style: wider streets with an [orthogonal](/wiki/Orthogonal "Orthogonal") layout. This part of the city is today traditionally regarded as the city center and is called *Nova Varoš* (*New town*).

During World War II, Podgorica was almost razed to the ground, being [bombed over 80 times](/wiki/Bombing_of_Podgorica_in_World_War_II "Bombing of Podgorica in World War II").^[\[17\]](#cite_note-Kovacevic57-20)^ After liberation, rebuilding began as in other cities of the communist-ruled SFRY. Mass residential blocks were erected, with basic design typical of [Eastern bloc](/wiki/Eastern_bloc "Eastern bloc") countries. ^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^ All that part of the city on the right bank of the [Morača River](/wiki/Mora%C4%8Da_River "Morača River") was built this way.{#cite_ref-Kovacevic57_20-1}

The main contemporary traffic arteries were laid out during this period, which extended the orthogonal street layout of the city center, to the south and west. Residential and infrastructural developments in the SFRY era have mostly shaped the layout of today's Podgorica and accommodated the unprecedented population growth that followed World War II. ^\[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]^

A major advance in Podgorica architecture began in the late 1990s and, since then, the face of the city has changed rapidly. Residential and business construction are proceeding rapidly, incorporating contemporary glass-and-steel architectural trends. In an effort to create a recognizable and modern state capital, city officials are routing significant investments in the city's public spaces. Thus, the city has gained entirely new squares and some monuments. New landmarks include the *Hristovog Vaskrsenja* [orthodox temple](/wiki/Orthodox_temple_(church) "Orthodox temple (church)") and the [Millennium Bridge](/wiki/Millennium_Bridge,_Podgorica "Millennium Bridge, Podgorica"), the main feature of the Podgorica skyline.
